GE Vernova, the energy business spun out of General Electric, sits squarely in the path of the power buildout that AI demands. Its gas power segment supplies the heavy-duty turbines that utilities and, increasingly, data center developers are ordering to quickly add generation capacity. Its electrification and grid businesses provide the transformers, switchgear, and grid-modernization equipment needed to move all of that new power to where it is consumed. When a hyperscaler announces a multi-gigawatt campus, equipment like GE Vernova's is being ordered somewhere down the supply chain. What makes the GE Vernova story distinct is what it does not carry. Investors who want exposure to AI compute growth through chips or memory must accept brutal cyclicality, inventory swings, and pricing cycles. Power infrastructure demand behaves differently. Turbine orders come with multi-year delivery schedules. Grid equipment is bought against decade-long utility planning cycles. Service agreements generate recurring revenue for the life of the installed equipment. The result is a business with far greater revenue visibility than the semiconductor complex, which is attached to the same underlying demand driver. And the company’s fundamentals absolutely back up the story and recent share performance. GEV posted its Q1 2026 results on April 22, topping earnings estimates by $1.95 per share while quarterly revenue of $9.34 billion grew 17% year-over-year. The company delivered strong orders, raised 2026 guidance across all key metrics, and saw revenue and backlog growth in both equipment and its services segments. Analysts project earnings growth of over 62% for the year ahead, among the strongest of any large-cap industrial. The balance sheet is clean, with a debt-to-equity ratio of just 0.19. Title: What Could Really Power Microsoft Stock Higher From Here?

What looks like another AI product is actually a fundamental and potentially lucrative shift in how the company makes its money. For a company at the center of the AI universe, Microsoft (MSFT) stock has been surprisingly earthbound lately. It’s down about 20% over the past year and trades 28% below its 52-week high. With so much attention on new features, you might be wondering what could actually provide the fuel for a sustained climb from here. The answer lies in a quiet but profound shift in the company’s entire business model, one that could unlock a new layer of growth on top of its substantial existing customer base. For decades, Microsoft sold you software. Now, it’s moving to a model where it sells you outcomes. The company’s own management describes the transformation as moving to a “per user and usage business.” Think about that. It’s one thing to sell a subscription for access to a tool; it’s another thing entirely to get paid for every single task that tool performs. The goal extends beyond simply adding more seats to capturing a fraction of the value from the billions of queries, reports, and summaries its AI agents will run. This model, if successful, could fundamentally re-rate what each of Microsoft’s hundreds of millions of users is worth. A great strategy is one thing, but execution is another. The early evidence for this new model comes from the front lines of its AI push: Microsoft 365 Copilot. The company now has “over 20 million Microsoft 365 Copilot paid seats,” a number that’s accelerating. In the most recent quarter, seat additions increased 250% year-over-year, representing the fastest growth since the product’s launch. This isn’t a hypothetical; it’s a real, paying customer base that is rapidly adopting the very consumption-based products that represent the company’s future. Microsoft is backing this strategic shift with an immense amount of capital. The company expects to invest roughly $190 billion in capital expenditures in calendar year 2026 alone. That significant figure is allocated for building the global engine required to power all that new, paid usage, a task that goes far beyond constructing more data centers. The company has been clear that even with this spending, strong customer demand continues to exceed available capacity. It’s a classic growth story: invest heavily to meet overwhelming demand that you can directly monetize. This spending is the clearest signal of management’s conviction in the per-user, per-usage future. Microsoft is undertaking more than another product cycle; the company is attempting to rewire its entire commercial relationship with its customers. The bet is that by embedding AI agents into the daily workflow of nearly every office worker on the planet, it can create a new, durable, and high-margin revenue stream that compounds for years to come. The pieces are in place, the spending is committed, and the first wave of customers is already paying up. An opportunity like this only counts once it starts showing up in the numbers, and the first hard place it surfaces is management’s guidance.
