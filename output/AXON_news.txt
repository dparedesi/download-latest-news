Tenet Healthcare regains full ownership of subsidiary Conifer Health Solutions by unwinding its joint venture with CommonSpirit Health. Conifer will pay $540 million to redeem CommonSpirit’s 24% stake, while CommonSpirit will pay Tenet $1.9 billion over three years in exit obligations. CommonSpirit will continue receiving RCM services from Conifer through the end of 2026, after which it plans to transition away from Conifer. Conifer has provided RCM services to CommonSpirit and and its predecessor organization, Catholic Health Initiatives, since 2012. Contact us. “A simple search on the named authors (when presented) reveals another carefully concealed attempt at Epic influence…” The site is called “epicresearch.org”, it has a navigation bar at the top to the rest of Epic’s public websites, and it uses the standard Epic branding. I’m not sure I see the careful attempt at concealment here? Additionally, I looked through the articles they’ve published since the beginning of 2025 and all but 2 of them list the study’s authors. For the two that don’t, they’re simple comments on trends in the data and aren’t really drawing inferences or conclusions from it (one was “hand foot and mouth cases are going up” and the other was “telehealth usage is stable”). I do find Mr. H’s comment interesting. The data set used is from a subset of Epic customers who have chosen to contribute. If the organizations that contribute to the data set are not representative of Epic orgs in general (and/or if Epic orgs are not representative of healthcare organizations in general), then the results from examining that data set are less generalizable. This (of course) is a consideration with all medical research, and I’m not personally convinced that it actually would be a bigger issue here than it is with more traditional peer-reviewer publication paths. Monday, February 2, 2026 OpenAI (Hacker News): Today, we’re introducing the Codex app for macOS—a powerful new interface designed to effortlessly manage multiple agents at once, run work in parallel, and collaborate with agents over long-running tasks. We’re also excited to show more people what’s now possible with Codex. For a limited time we’re including Codex with ChatGPT Free and Go, and we’re doubling the rate limits on Plus, Pro, Business, Enterprise, and Edu plans. Those higher limits apply everywhere you use Codex—in the app, from the CLI, in your IDE, and in the cloud. The Codex app changes how software gets built and who can build it—from pairing with a single coding agent on targeted edits to supervising coordinated teams of agents across the full lifecycle of designing, building, shipping, and maintaining software. Samuel Axon: Skills—basically extensions in the form of folders filled with instructions and other resources—are also supported. The app lets users configure Automations, which follow instructions on a user-set schedule, with Skills support. Based on my time using Codex, it seems capable, even though OpenAI has been running a few months behind Anthropic on the product side. To help bridge the gap, OpenAI is using a strategy it has used before: higher usage limits at a similar cost. David Gewirtz: In addition to the app itself, OpenAI announced a new plan mode for Codex that allows for a read-only review (meaning the AI won’t muck with your code) and selectable personalities. Personally, I’ve had just about enough personality from the human programmers I’ve managed, so I’d prefer a nice, personality-free personality in my coding agent. Recently, OpenAI also announced that Codex has an IDE extension for use in the JetBrains IDEs. Readers may recall that back in June I moved off of PhpStorm, my favorite JetBrains development environment. I moved to VS Code simply because the AI tools were more available for that environment. It’s nice to see JetBrains IDE availability for those of us who prefer it over VS Code.
