Title: Google and Meta reportedly strike new, multibillion-dollar AI chip deal
URL: https://siliconangle.com/2026/02/26/google-meta-reportedly-strike-new-multibillion-dollar-ai-chip-deal/
Time Published: 2026-02-27T02:08:48Z
Full Content:
UPDATED 21:08 EST / FEBRUARY 26 2026 by Mike Wheatley Meta Platforms Inc. has reportedly agreed to a multibillion-dollar deal to rent Google Cloud’s custom artificial intelligence chips, according to a report in The Information today. The report cited anonymous sources as saying that the chips, known as tensor processing units, will be used by Meta to train and run its next-generation large language models. Large enterprises like Meta have been lavishing billions of dollars on advanced processors as they race to build up the infrastructure they need to power AI workloads, and Google has identified it as a significant opportunity for growth. The AI chip industry is currently dominated by Nvidia Corp.’s graphics processing units, which power the vast majority of AI applications in the world today, but Google’s TPUs are a compelling, lower-cost alternative. In recent years, TPUs have become one of the most important growth engines for Google’s cloud infrastructure platform, and the company believes there’s a real opportunity to increase its market share. Google launched its most advanced TPUs, called Ironwood, in November. Customers have the option to scale up to 9,216 Ironwood TPUs into a single server pod that’s linked by high-speed interconnects to provide up to 9.6 terabits per second of bandwidth. The chips can be connected to a colossal 1.77 petabytes of shared high-bandwidth memory or HBM. According to Google, the Ironwood chips can deliver more than 118 times the FP8 ExaFLOPS of its nearest competitor and four-times better performance for training and inference than Trillium, its previous generation TPU. One of the first companies to adopt the new TPUs was Anthropic PBC, which touted the massive price-performance gains they provide, enabling it to serve massive Claude models at scale. Earlier, Google announced it had struck a deal with Anthropic worth “tens of billions of dollars” to give it access to 1 million TPUs through its cloud infrastructure platform. Today’s deal is important for Meta as it looks to diversify its AI hardware away from Nvidia. The social media giant is one of Nvidia’s biggest customers, and earlier this month announced a multibillion-dollar deal to buy millions of the company’s next-generation Vera Rubin GPUs when they become available later this year. But Meta doesn’t want to be tied to a single chip provider, and has also established a relationship with one of Nvidia’s biggest rivals in Advanced Micro Devices Inc. Earlier this week, the Facebook parent said it’s going to buy billions of dollars’ worth of AMD’s AI chips, including its newest Instinct MI400 series GPUs. In addition, Meta also received an option to buy a 10% stake in AMD, but that will only happen if its collaboration with the chipmaker meets agreed-upon performance milestones. Meta’s deal with Google allows it to diversify its AI hardware supplier base even more, and there are good reasons for it to want to do so. Each type of AI processors has its advantages and disadvantages, so the social media giant will be able to pair the optimal silicon with each kind of AI workload. In addition, by playing off different chipmakers against one another, it can potentially secure favorable prices as it looks to build out its AI infrastructure. Meanwhile, Google has ambitions to break Nvidia’s stranglehold on the AI chip market. It knows that enterprises want alternatives to Nvidia’s GPUs, and it’s determined to take advantage of that. Historically, its TPUs have only been available through the Google Cloud platform, meaning customers could only rent access to them. But now Google wants to sell the chips directly to customers so they can run them in their own, private data centers. By doing so, it believes it can grab up to 10% of Nvidia’s data center revenue in the next few years. The Information’s report said Meta is currently talking to Google about buying millions of TPUs for its own data centers. That would be a separate deal to today’s agreement, which only relates to cloud access. However, no agreement has been reached so far, the report said. Less clear is what today’s deal means for Meta’s own in-house chips. The company last updated its custom Meta Training and Inference Accelerator or MTIA chips in 2024, and has reportedly been working with Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. on a new design that was expected to launch sometime this year. The next-generation MTIA chips are reportedly optimized for AI model training, which is an area where its first-generation processors struggled. However, it’s believed that the company has been experiencing “technical challenges” with the new chips that appear to have delayed their rollout. Title: NVIDIA Stock vs AMD: Which Stock Is Better for Long-Term Investors?

Time Published: 2026-02-25T20:46:52Z
Full Content:
When investors search for NVIDIA stock, they’re usually asking a bigger question: Is NVIDIA the best semiconductor investment for the next decade or is AMD a smarter long-term bet? Both NVIDIA and Advanced Micro Devices sit at the center of the AI revolution. Both design high-performance chips that power artificial intelligence, data centers, gaming systems, cloud platforms, and advanced computing. But if you’re investing for 10, 20, or even 30 years, you need more than hype. You need fundamentals, growth visibility, profitability strength, and durable competitive advantages. This in-depth guide compares NVIDIA stock vs AMD stock across every major factor that matters to long-term investors — in simple, clear language. By the end, you’ll understand: Why NVIDIA stock has surged in recent years How AMD competes in CPUs and GPUs Which company has stronger financial performance The risks investors should watch Whether NVIDIA stock deserves its premium valuation And which stock may fit your long-term portfolio better Contents Over the past several years, NVIDIA stock has become one of the most talked-about investments in the world. The reason? Artificial intelligence. NVIDIA’s GPUs (graphics processing units) have become essential for training large AI models. These chips handle massive parallel processing tasks that traditional CPUs struggle with. As AI adoption accelerated, demand for NVIDIA hardware surged. Data centers, cloud providers, and AI startups all needed NVIDIA chips. That explosive demand fueled dramatic revenue growth — and NVIDIA stock responded accordingly. But here’s the key question for long-term investors: Is NVIDIA stock still a strong buy going forward, or has too much future growth already been priced in? To answer that, we must compare it directly with AMD. Before deciding which stock is better, you need to understand how each company makes money. NVIDIA generates revenue primarily from: Data center GPUs (AI training and inference) Gaming GPUs (GeForce) Professional visualization Automotive AI systems AI software ecosystems What makes NVIDIA stock unique is not just hardware dominance — it’s the ecosystem. Its CUDA platform allows developers to build AI applications optimized specifically for NVIDIA hardware. Once companies build systems around CUDA, switching becomes costly and complex. That creates a competitive moat. This ecosystem advantage is one reason NVIDIA stock trades at a premium. AMD operates across multiple semiconductor categories: Ryzen CPUs for desktops and laptops EPYC processors for servers Radeon GPUs for gaming Instinct accelerators for AI Unlike NVIDIA, AMD competes directly with Intel in CPUs while also competing with NVIDIA in GPUs. AMD’s strength lies in diversification. If one product segment slows, another may compensate. For investors who want broader semiconductor exposure, AMD stock offers that flexibility. Artificial intelligence is not a short-term trend. It represents a structural shift in computing. NVIDIA stock has benefited immensely from this shift because: NVIDIA dominates high-end AI GPUs. Many of the largest AI models are trained on NVIDIA hardware. Major cloud providers rely heavily on NVIDIA chips. This dominance gives NVIDIA strong pricing power and margin strength. AMD is expanding into AI accelerators, but it still trails NVIDIA in ecosystem strength and overall market share. For long-term investors, the key question becomes: Will NVIDIA maintain AI leadership, or will AMD capture meaningful share? If NVIDIA sustains dominance, NVIDIA stock may continue outperforming. Revenue growth often determines long-term stock performance. In recent years: NVIDIA revenue has surged due to AI-driven data center demand. AMD revenue has grown steadily but at a slower pace. NVIDIA’s growth acceleration has been particularly strong in data center segments. That growth directly impacts NVIDIA stock valuation. AMD’s growth has been more balanced across CPUs and GPUs, providing stability but not the same explosive AI-driven acceleration. If AI spending continues expanding rapidly, NVIDIA stock may retain a growth advantage. If AI growth normalizes, AMD could close the gap. Profit margins reveal how much pricing power a company has. NVIDIA has significantly higher gross margins than AMD. Why? Because NVIDIA sells premium products deeply integrated with proprietary software. Customers often prioritize performance and ecosystem compatibility over price. Higher margins mean: More free cash flow Greater R&D investment Stronger financial resilience Ability to weather downturns AMD’s margins are improving but remain lower. Competitive pricing can limit margin expansion. For long-term investors evaluating NVIDIA stock, margin strength is a major positive factor. A stock becomes a strong long-term investment when the company has durable competitive advantages. CUDA software ecosystem Developer loyalty High switching costs Dominant AI GPU market share Brand leadership in performance computing This moat supports the long-term case for NVIDIA stock. Strong CPU innovation Diversified product lineup Competitive pricing strategy Growing server adoption AMD’s moat is competitive strength across multiple segments, rather than dominance in one. Looking historically: NVIDIA stock has delivered extraordinary long-term returns, especially during the AI boom. AMD stock has also delivered strong gains, particularly after regaining CPU competitiveness. However, NVIDIA stock has significantly outperformed over the past decade. Past performance does not guarantee future returns — but it reflects execution quality and investor confidence. Valuation is critical. NVIDIA stock often trades at a premium price-to-earnings ratio compared to AMD. Investors are willing to pay that premium because of: AI leadership Revenue growth visibility Strong margins Ecosystem dominance However, premium valuations carry risk. If growth slows, valuation multiples can compress. AMD sometimes trades at high multiples as well, especially when growth expectations increase. Long-term investors must weigh growth potential against current price levels. Every stock carries risk. AI demand could slow. Government export restrictions could impact sales. Competitors may close the performance gap. Market expectations may be too high. Continued NVIDIA dominance in AI. Competitive pressure from Intel. Lower margin cushion during downturns. Execution risks in AI expansion. Semiconductor stocks are cyclical. Supply-demand imbalances can create volatility. Long-term investors should expect fluctuations. NVIDIA Stock NVIDIA stock is heavily weighted toward GPUs and AI infrastructure. AMD stock offers more balanced exposure between CPUs and GPUs. If AI dominates future computing, NVIDIA stock could benefit more. If CPU competition intensifies and AMD gains share, AMD stock could see strong upside. It comes down to whether you prefer focused dominance or diversified competition. The semiconductor industry is foundational to modern society. Future growth drivers include: Artificial intelligence Autonomous vehicles Robotics Cloud computing Edge computing Advanced gaming Enterprise digital transformation Both NVIDIA and AMD operate in expanding markets. The key long-term question is which company captures more of that growth. Let’s consider three possible futures. If AI infrastructure spending expands aggressively and NVIDIA maintains leadership, NVIDIA stock may outperform significantly. If AMD gains share and AI growth remains strong but more competitive, AMD stock could generate higher percentage returns from a smaller base. If semiconductor demand softens cyclically, NVIDIA’s stronger margins may provide greater resilience. Choose NVIDIA stock if you: Prefer market leaders Believe in sustained AI dominance Value strong margins Accept premium valuations Choose AMD stock if you: Want diversified semiconductor exposure Believe AMD can gain market share Prefer potential upside from competitive growth Are comfortable with more balanced exposure Some long-term investors hold both stocks to diversify within the semiconductor space. If you’re considering NVIDIA stock for the long term: Avoid trying to time short-term volatility. Consider dollar-cost averaging. Monitor AI spending trends. Watch earnings growth and margins. Maintain diversification beyond one stock. Technology stocks can experience rapid swings. Long-term conviction matters. NVIDIA stock represents AI leadership, strong profitability, and ecosystem dominance. AMD stock represents diversified semiconductor exposure and competitive growth potential. If you believe AI will continue reshaping global computing and NVIDIA will maintain its leadership position, NVIDIA stock may be the stronger long-term choice. If you prefer diversified chip exposure and believe competition will narrow the gap, AMD stock could offer compelling upside. Both companies operate in expanding industries with significant long-term potential. The better stock ultimately depends on your risk tolerance, investment timeline, and conviction in AI’s future trajectory. For many investors, the most strategic approach may not be choosing one over the other — but understanding how each fits within a diversified, long-term portfolio strategy.
