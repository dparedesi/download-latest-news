As fears of a recession and interest rate cuts from the European Central Bank have subsided, investors have turned even more positive towards European banks, revising up their expectations for the sector, despite a complicated backdrop. Meanwhile, AI has emerged as a new force drawing investors to European lenders' shares, partly because a dearth of technology companies in the region has forced many to hunt for AI beneficiaries in old-economy markets. Banks have started to use AI to improve operational efficiency and fraud detection, as well as to reduce staff costs. "European banks could be a real beneficiary of AI," said Helen Jewell, chief investment officer for fundamental equities at BlackRock, the world's largest asset manager, with about $12 trillion under management. UBS said in a note to investors they see AI as a key source of potential upside to banks' near-term valuations and longer- term earnings. But that comes with risks. And risks aren't only AI-related. Yet investors have snapped up bank stocks in earnest. Societe Generale shares have rallied 140% this year, Commerzbank 125% and those in Barclays rose almost 70%. An index of European bank stocks is up more than 60%, on top of the 25% gain in 2024 and more than four times better than the pan European index. Investors also view them as relatively cheap, particularly when compared with U.S. banking shares. European bank stocks currently trade around 1.17 times their price-to-book value, some 40% below their 2007 peak and below the 1.7 times of their U.S. rivals, according to LSEG data. Consulting firm McKinsey estimated last year that AI could bring the global banking industry as much as $340 billion a year in additional value, with a drop in operational costs of 20%. Last month, analysts raised their net revisions for the sector by the most since May 2023, and 12-month forward earnings growth expectations jumped to the highest since 2023, according to IBES data. Growth in bank lending to euro zone firms is still running near its highest since mid-2023, according to the most recent ECB data. Credit growth to businesses was unchanged at 2.9% in October - just below August's 3%, the most since May 2023 - compared to the previous month, while loan growth to households accelerated to a 2-1/2-year high of 2.8% from 2.6%. Title: Is testosterone the quick fix for women in midlife?

This research-backed, evidence-based medical view is not anti-testosterone; it‚Äôs just anti-magic. In her own 800-woman trial of a testosterone patch, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, women on testosterone had better sexual function than those on placebo. But when the team buried validated wellbeing questionnaires inside the study, they found no difference in general wellbeing at all. Hartley encounters the anecdotal power of testosterone every day. ‚ÄúI have patients who will come back and say, ‚ÄòI went on testosterone and my brain fog improved‚Äô ‚Äì and I am not in their brain, I cannot tell them that they are wrong.‚Äù The problem is that individual stories, which are very real to the woman telling them, are not the same as evidence. ‚ÄúHow do you remove all the other things that may have helped? How do we know it is not placebo?‚Äù Hartley asks. ‚ÄúThat is why we do studies.‚Äù But when women are looking for answers and hear one or two stories saying, ‚ÄòThis transformed my life‚Äô, the effect is infectious. ‚ÄúIt is human nature to want to believe it,‚Äù Hartley says. This gap between evidence and promise is sharpened by what happens outside regulated medical practice. In the US, an increasingly booming, unregulated industry of influencers and wellness clinics is offering female clients testosterone pellets and gels at doses that take women well beyond a normal female range for the hormone. At those levels, the side effects can be alarming: scalp hair loss, unwanted facial hair, acne, irritability, voice deepening, clitoral enlargement, and surges of arousal that disrupt daily life. Some of these side effects, like clitoral enlargement and voice changes, can be irreversible. Hartley notes that such dramatic changes ‚Äúreally should only occur if your testosterone level is bumped outside of a normal female range‚Äù ‚Äì yet in unregulated settings, that boundary is routinely crossed. In countries like the United Kingdom or Australia, women have access to a licensed female-specific testosterone cream that allows safe, standardised dosing. In countries like the US or Ireland, without such a product, the gap between evidence-based prescribing and unregulated hormonal experimentation is widening, leaving women vulnerable to treatments that promise transformation but bypass the safeguards of medical care. ‚ÄúKnowing that this is an option that might help them, women are forced to use either compounded therapies or try microdose male options,‚Äù Davis says of countries without an approved product. ‚ÄúThat is really where things go out the window, because it is very difficult to safely dose using very concentrated male formulations.‚Äù In her view, ‚Äúthe best thing to protect women is a regulated product‚Äù, one tailored to female physiology. Hartley would welcome the UK‚Äôs new female-specific cream for similar reasons. It contains the same active ingredient as existing male gels, she notes, just at a concentration and formulation that makes it easier to apply in tiny doses. Regulation, though, is not enough on its own ‚Äì doctors need to understand the drug and how best to help their patients. For Hartley, the safeguard is information. ‚ÄúThe more we have well-educated healthcare professionals at every level,‚Äù she says, ‚Äúthe more we are passing that on to women.‚Äù She wants GPs and specialists who are ‚Äúwell armed with information on what is evidence-based and what is rubbish‚Äù, so that they can help patients sift through hype and anecdote. Both Hartley and Davis think the fervour around testosterone says as much about the state of women‚Äôs lives as it does about the hormone itself. ‚ÄúI think women are sick of their symptoms being dismissed,‚Äù Davis says. ‚ÄúAnd when someone says this will make you feel better, of course, they want to take it. I get that, and I think that is perfectly reasonable.‚Äù Davis‚Äôs team has documented the decline in women‚Äôs quality of life at mid-age. Despite a huge amount of cultural discourse focusing on the so-called male loneliness epidemic, the documented mental health struggles of middle-aged women remain unacknowledged. ‚ÄúWomen are suffering,‚Äù she says. ‚ÄúWe have shown in our latest work that midlife wellbeing has gone down compared to 10 years ago. There is a real decline.‚Äù Davis explains the multitude of factors affecting women, noting: ‚ÄúThis is the sandwich generation. Women trying to ‚Äòhave it all‚Äô: have children, take care of ageing parents, have work, have work-life balance, have financial stability. These are enormous pressures.‚Äù Depression rates are high, distress is high, and time is scarce. Hartley is watching the same pressures play out in Irish consulting rooms. ‚ÄúYou see how much women are carrying,‚Äù she says. ‚ÄúIt is no wonder their libido is low or that they feel flat or foggy or depleted. That is not a pathology. It is life.‚Äù Amid all the stresses affecting women, it thus becomes necessary to be careful of how we speak about women and low libido, in order not to pathologise a lack of desire that may be natural or due to a host of interlocking external factors, or to risk reinforcing damaging ideas about women owing their bodies and sexual availability to others. Neither doctor wants to minimise the distress of women whose sexual desire has vanished. Both prescribe testosterone in carefully selected cases, understanding the stakes for relationships and for individual self-esteem. ‚ÄúOur own research has shown that if women themselves personally have poor sexual wellbeing, that impacts them personally in their general wellbeing and their relationships,‚Äù Davis says. ‚ÄúSo I prescribe testosterone for women with low libido. I am not anti-testosterone.‚Äù But what does concern Davis and Hartley is how the issue of women‚Äôs libido is often medicalised or framed. Hartley notes how many women come in apologising, or framing their lack of desire as a personal failure or a dereliction of duty to their partners. ‚ÄúWomen come in saying, ‚ÄòOh my God, my poor husband, my poor partner‚Äô,‚Äù says Hartley, who is always clear in redirecting them. ‚ÄúI say, ‚ÄòYour partner is not my patient‚Äô.‚Äù The real question is whether the woman herself is distressed. ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs really important to not stick it on the list as something that has to be fixed. Low libido is not a disease,‚Äù says Hartley, who notes that it is a normal evolution for spontaneous sexual desire to reduce as you get older. ‚ÄùThat is universal. There is nothing wrong with you if that happens.‚Äù Davis notes that often, low libido is a ‚Äúmultifactorial condition‚Äù, and she points out that the guidelines from the International Society for the Study of Women‚Äôs Sexual Health set out a process of care that requires clinicians to look first at depression, relationship dynamics, medications, trauma, pain and vaginal health before they ‚Äújump in saying testosterone is going to be the fix‚Äù. Underpinning all of this, both the hope and the caution, is a simple fact: there is not enough research into women‚Äôs health, which leaves us without the data that women deserve. ‚ÄúThe future, hopefully, is research,‚Äù Hartley says. Without larger, longer, better trials, she and her colleagues cannot give women the clear answers they want about benefits and risks. Davis has spent four decades trying to generate that data and sounds, at times, weary. Getting funding is brutally hard, she says; she can spend ‚Äúup to 30 per cent‚Äù of her working time writing grant applications. When money does come through, another hurdle appears. ‚ÄúTrying to get women to go into clinical trials is an absolute no,‚Äù she says. ‚ÄúThey are all too busy, busy.‚Äù Her team is about to start two large new studies, one looking at testosterone‚Äôs effects on muscle and bone, the other at bone and sexual function, both including wellbeing questionnaires. She welcomes the increased interest but insists that until the trials report, the honest answer to many questions will remain ‚Äúwe do not know‚Äù. That is not a comfortable message in a culture that wants a quick fix for midlife female exhaustion and desire. It is, however, the only message that treats women as adults and demands better for them in the long term. So what should an Irish woman do, sifting through a wave of hyped-up Instagram adverts about testosterone pellets and listicles of anecdotes about life-changing results? ‚ÄúKeep expectations realistic,‚Äù Davis says. If you are going to try testosterone, she urges, treat it as a time-limited trial, not a new identity. Do not try to treat bone loss, muscle fatigue or life itself with testosterone. ‚ÄúTestosterone is not the answer for life.‚Äù Hartley, for her part, hopes women will come in to talk, not arrive convinced that there is only one solution. ‚ÄúIf they are symptomatic, if they have low libido and it is bothering them, and they want to have a discussion with their GP about it, do that,‚Äù she says. But she also wants them to hear something that often gets lost in the hype: ‚ÄúYou are not broken.‚Äù She‚Äôs right. There is nothing inherently wrong about wanting sex less as you age. There is also nothing shameful about wanting more. But there is something deeply shameful about a society that devalues women; refuses to invest in research about their physical or emotional wellbeing; pathologises them for ageing and constantly tells them that they are not enough, only to try then make money selling them an unsafe, unregulated ‚Äúcure‚Äù. Women deserve acceptance, accurate information and autonomy, not another marketplace built on their insecurity. ¬© 2025 The Irish Times DAC
