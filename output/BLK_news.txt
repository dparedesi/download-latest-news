Title: Global Trade at Gunpoint

President Donald Trump stood behind a podium in a large clearing of the Rose Garden, American flags hanging vertically between each White House column, to lambast a global trade system in which ‚Äã‚Äúour country has been looted, pillaged, raped and plundered by nations near and far, both friend and foe alike.‚Äù It was April 2, which Trump deemed ‚Äã‚ÄúLiberation Day,‚Äù and he was declaring ‚Äã‚Äúeconomic independence‚Äù from the rest of the world. About five minutes into the announcement, Trump started waving what he called a ‚Äã‚Äúspecial book‚Äù back and forth, like a prosecutor holding up evidence in a courtroom. The misdeeds against the United States, he said, are ‚Äã‚Äúdetailed in a very big report by the U.S. Trade Representative.‚Äù The document, the 2025 National Trade Estimate Report on Foreign Trade Barriers, is a 377-page laundry list of what the United States views as ‚Äã‚Äútrade barriers‚Äù but what people in other countries might see as legitimate protections and regulations. In the same executive order that established ‚Äã‚Äúreciprocal tariffs‚Äù on April 2, Trump was explicit about setting up a protection racket. The order states that the United States may decide to reduce tariffs if a country changes its policies to ‚Äã‚Äúalign sufficiently with the United States on economic and national security matters.‚Äù For Trump‚Äôs Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR), it‚Äôs a problem that South Korea prioritizes domestic technology for its own weapons production.
