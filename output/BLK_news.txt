The winds of innovation have always swept through our capital markets, often at gale force. In 1792, they rustled the leaves of a buttonwood tree, beneath which two dozen stockbrokers assembled to establish the forerunner to the New York Stock Exchange. That modest agreement‚Äîfewer than a hundred words handwritten on a slip of parchment‚Äîset in motion an elegant design that would govern the flow of capital for generations.[2] In the centuries since, our markets have never stood still. They have expanded, evolved, and reinvented themselves in step with the ideas and technologies of their time. Markets are dynamic because of the people who participate in them. Markets channel human ingenuity toward society‚Äôs most intractable problems by rewarding those who develop the most innovative solutions that others value enough to buy. They are the mechanism by which Adam Smith‚Äôs invisible hand elevates those who act in the common good‚Äîeven when pursuing their own. The SEC‚Äôs role is to safeguard markets that allow the spark of human creativity and skill to benefit society. Over the arc of its history, the agency has both enabled innovation and, regrettably at times, stifled it. Fortunately, progress has a way of prevailing. And when our regulatory posture is calibrated to meet innovation with thoughtfulness rather than fear, America‚Äôs leadership position has only grown stronger. *** In the 1960s‚Äîbefore my time, I am happy to say‚ÄîWall Street was riding a bull market. But behind the scenes, our market machinery was straining to keep up. Most clearing and settlement transactions involved a costly and cumbersome process. Rising stacks of paper stock certificates had to be physically delivered by clerks wheeling carts up and down Wall Street and in other financial districts all across America.[3] It was a scene from another century struggling to meet the demands of the modern securities markets. Indeed, the paper-based clearance and settlement systems, built for a gentler era, began to buckle under the weight of soaring volumes. Delays at one firm held up the work of another. Securities were lost or stolen. Fails ballooned. And many thinly capitalized broker-dealers were caught by the whiplash of scuttled transactions. In desperation, trading hours were reduced and exchanges eventually closed on Wednesdays to allow firms to process the mountains of certificates. So today, I would like the world to go on notice that under my leadership, the SEC will not stand idly by and watch innovations develop overseas while our capital markets remain stagnant. To achieve President Trump‚Äôs vision of making America the crypto capital of the world, the SEC must holistically consider the potential benefits and risks of moving our markets from an off-chain environment to an on-chain one. We are at the threshold of a new era in the history of our markets. As I mentioned earlier, today I am announcing the launch of ‚ÄúProject Crypto‚Äù‚Äîa Commission-wide initiative to modernize the securities rules and regulations to enable America‚Äôs financial markets to move on-chain. Just a few weeks ago, President Trump signed the GENIUS Act into law, ensuring that America will continue to lead in global payments with a gold standard stablecoin regulatory framework. Upon signing the GENIUS Act into law, I was pleased that President Trump endorsed Congressional efforts to pass crypto market structure legislation by the end of the year. I commend the House of Representatives for garnering such strong bipartisan support, and I look forward to working with the Senate as they build off the House‚Äôs work and craft market structure legislation that future proofs our markets against regulatory mischief, cementing the United States as the crypto capital of the world. Yesterday, the President‚Äôs Working Group on Digital Asset Markets released the PWG Report with clear recommendations for the SEC and other federal agencies to build a framework to maintain U.S. dominance in crypto asset markets. This report is the blueprint to make America first in blockchain and crypto technology. The President said last week that he wants ‚Äúthe entire world running on the backbone of American technology.‚Äù[9] I stand ready to help get that job done. That is why I am launching Project Crypto and directing the SEC‚Äôs policy divisions to work with the Crypto Task Force, led by Commissioner Peirce, to swiftly develop proposals to implement the PWG‚Äôs recommendations. Project Crypto will help ensure that the United States remains the best place in the world to start a business, develop cutting-edge technologies, and participate in capital markets. We will reshore the crypto businesses that fled our country, particularly those that were crippled by the previous administration‚Äôs regulation-by-enforcement crusade and ‚ÄúOperation Chokepoint 2.0‚Äù[10] Whether an incumbent or a new entrant, the SEC welcomes all market participants who are hungry to innovate. In accord with the PWG Report‚Äôs recommendations, I have directed the Commission staff to draft clear and simple rules of the road for crypto asset distributions, custody, and trading for public notice and comment. While the Commission staff works to finalize these regulations, the Commission and its staff will in the coming months consider using interpretative, exemptive, and other authorities to make sure that archaic rules and regulations do not smother innovation and entrepreneurship in America. Many of the Commission‚Äôs legacy rules and regulations do not make sense in the twenty-first century‚Äîlet alone for on-chain markets. The Commission must revamp its rulebook so that regulatory moats do not hinder progress and competition‚Äîfrom both new entrants and incumbents‚Äîto the detriment of Main Street.[11] Now, Project Crypto will involve a broad range of initiatives across the Commission. First, we will work to bring crypto asset distributions back to America. The days of convoluted offshore corporate structures, decentralization theater, and confusion over security status, are over. President Trump has said that America is in its Golden Age‚Äîand under our new agenda, our crypto asset economy will be, too. In line with the PWG Report, a key priority of mine will be to establish‚Äîas swiftly as we can‚Äîa regulatory framework for distributions of crypto assets in America. Capital formation is at the heart of the SEC‚Äôs mission, yet for too long the SEC ignored market demands for choice and disincentivized crypto-based capital raising.[12] As a result, crypto markets pivoted away from offering crypto assets and deprived investors of the opportunity to use this technology to contribute to productive economic enterprises. The SEC‚Äôs head-in-the-sand posture‚Äîas well as its shoot first, ask questions later approach‚Äîare days of the past. Despite what the SEC has said in the past, most crypto assets are not securities. But confusion over the application of the ‚ÄúHowey test‚Äù has led some innovators to prophylactically treat all crypto assets as such. American entrepreneurs are harnessing blockchain technology to modernize a broad range of legacy systems and instruments. One such entrepreneur is Senator Bernie Moreno of Ohio, a successful businessman and freshman senator, who before his election to the Senate founded a company that put car titles on the blockchain.[13] He saw a need for efficiency in transferring titles and devised a practical solution with the new technology. These entrepreneurs need‚Äîand deserve‚Äîbright-line rules for determining whether the securities laws apply to their businesses. I have directed the Commission staff to work to develop clear guidelines that market participants can use to determine whether a crypto asset is a security or subject to an investment contract. Our goal is to help market participants to slot crypto assets into categories, such as digital collectibles, digital commodities, or stablecoins, and assess the economic realities of a transaction. This approach can allow market participants to determine, based upon clear guidelines, whether any outstanding promises or commitments of the issuer cause the crypto asset to be subject to an investment contract. In addition, it should not be a scarlet letter to be deemed a security. We need a regulatory framework for crypto asset securities that allows these products to flourish within American markets. Many issuers will prefer the flexibility in product design that the securities laws afford, and investors will benefit from the opportunity to earn distributions, voting rights, and other features typical of securities. Projects should not be forced to establish decentralized autonomous organizations and offshore foundations or decentralize too early if this is not their desired plan of action. I am excited to see new use cases for crypto asset securities in commerce, such as the ability to participate in blockchain network consensus with tokenized equities. Thus, for those crypto asset transactions that are subject to the securities laws, I have asked staff to propose purpose-fit disclosures, exemptions, and safe harbors, including for so-called ‚Äúinitial coin offerings,‚Äù ‚Äúairdrops,‚Äù and network rewards. Regarding these sorts of transactions, our goal should be that issuers no longer exclude Americans from their distributions to avoid legal complexity and lawsuits,[14] but instead choose to include Americans to enjoy legal certainty and an accommodating regulatory environment. It is my view that a Cambrian explosion in innovation could occur if we stay true to this course. Additionally, many firms seek to ‚Äútokenize‚Äù their common stock, bonds, partnership interests, and other securities, or tokenize the securities of third parties.[15] Much of this innovation is offshore today due to regulatory challenges in the United States. I also hear from our regulatory policy staff that firms‚Äîfrom household names on Wall Street to unicorn tech companies in Silicon Valley‚Äîare lined up at our doors with requests to tokenize. I have asked the Commission staff to work with firms seeking to distribute tokenized securities within the United States and to provide relief where appropriate to assure that Americans are not left behind. Second, to achieve the President‚Äôs goals, it is incumbent on the SEC to ensure that market participants have maximum choice when deciding where to custody and trade crypto assets. As I have said before, the right to have self-custody of one‚Äôs private property is a core American value.[16] I believe deeply in the right to use a self-custodial digital wallet to maintain personal crypto assets and participate in on-chain activities like staking. However, some investors will continue to rely on SEC registrants, such as broker-dealers and investment advisers, to hold assets on their behalf, and these firms are subject to additional regulatory requirements when they do so. It will be a priority of my chairmanship to carry out the PWG Report‚Äôs recommendation to modernize the SEC‚Äôs custody requirements for registered intermediaries. The prior Administration‚Äôs ‚Äúspecial-purpose broker-dealer‚Äù framework, SAB 121, and ‚ÄúOperation Chokepoint 2.0,‚Äù resulted in a dearth of custodial service provider options in the market today.[17] The existing custody rules were created without crypto assets in mind. I have directed the staff to consider how best to adapt the existing regime to facilitate the custody of crypto assets, including possible exemptive or other relief, in addition to changes to the rules themselves. As the PWG Report recommends, market participants ‚Äúshould be permitted to engage in multiple business lines under the most efficient licensing structure possible.‚Äù We should not force market participants to be stretched to fit a Procrustean bed of regulation for regulation‚Äôs sake. I am in favor of affording them the freedom to choose the most efficient regulatory framework for their business, provided that the framework adequately protects investors. Third, a key priority of my chairmanship is to allow market participants to innovate with ‚Äúsuper-apps.‚Äù[18] I am often asked, ‚ÄúWhat do you mean by a super-app?‚Äù Plain and simple: securities intermediaries should be able to offer a broad range of products and services under one roof with a single license. A broker-dealer with an alternative trading system should be able to offer trading in non-security crypto assets alongside crypto asset securities, traditional securities, and other services, like crypto asset staking and lending, without requiring fifty-plus state licenses or multiple federal licenses. Nothing in the federal securities laws prohibits SEC-registered trading venues from listing non-securities on their platforms today, and I have directed the Commission staff to develop further guidance and proposals ultimately to make this ‚Äúsuper-app‚Äù vision a reality. Maybe they‚Äôll call it ‚ÄúReg Super-App.‚Äù Consistent with the PWG Report, the SEC in concert with other regulators should strive to have the most efficient licensing structure for SEC registrants. They should not be unnecessarily subject to multiple regulators or regulatory regimes. This model has worked well for banks, which are broadly exempted from many duplicative regulatory frameworks, such as broker-dealer and clearing agency registration. Regulators should provide the minimum effective dose of regulation necessary to protect investors while allowing entrepreneurs and businesses to flourish. We should not overburden them with paternalistic regulation that could drive them offshore or make American companies less competitive internationally. Our regulators should unleash the forces of venue and product competition for the benefit of all Americans. We should not artificially constrain business models and impose duplicative regulatory costs on American businesses that favor the largest firms that are better able to bear the regulatory burdens. Per the PWG‚Äôs recommendations, I have directed the Commission staff to develop a framework that will allow non-security crypto assets and crypto asset securities to be traded side-by-side on SEC-regulated platforms. Additionally, I have asked the staff to evaluate the use of Commission authority to permit non-security crypto assets that are subject to an investment contract to trade on trading venues that are not registered with the Commission. I am keen to pursue such a solution, as it will not only enable state-licensed crypto asset platforms that are not registered with the SEC to list certain crypto assets, but it also clears the way for CFTC-regulated platforms to offer these products with margin capabilities‚Äîeven without Congress providing the CFTC with any additional authority, unlocking even greater liquidity for these assets. Fourth, I have directed the Commission staff to update antiquated agency rules and regulations to unleash the potential of on-chain software systems in our securities markets. On-chain software comes in many shapes and sizes‚Äîsome of these systems are truly decentralized and not operated by any intermediary. Other on-chain software systems have an operator. Both types of on-chain software should have a place within our financial markets. It is essential that any crypto asset regulatory market structure create a path for software developers to unleash on-chain software systems that do not require operation by any central intermediary. Decentralized finance software systems‚Äîlike automated market makers‚Äîfacilitate automated, non-intermediated financial market activity. Federal securities laws have always assumed the involvement of intermediaries that require regulation, but this does not mean that we should interpose intermediaries for the sake of forcing intermediation where the markets can function without them. We will create space in our markets for both models, by protecting pure publishers of software code, drawing reasonable lines to distinguish intermediated and disintermediated activity, and creating rational and workable rules of the road for intermediaries that seek to operate on-chain software systems. Decentralized finance and other forms of on-chain software systems will be part of our securities markets and not drowned out by duplicative or unnecessary regulation. To make this vision a reality, we will need to consider some changes to our rules. For example, accommodating trading of tokenized securities on-chain may require us to explore amendments to Reg NMS, in addition to what we otherwise would do in the normal course to correct market distortions that it engenders. Many of you will remember that I co-authored with Commissioner Cynthia Glassman a lengthy dissent to the adoption of Reg NMS twenty years ago last month.[19] This dissent is even more compelling now that we have had two decades of prescriptive requirements that distort market activity and impede the evolution of our securities markets. Congress clearly intended that ‚Äúcompetitive forces, rather than unnecessary regulation, guide the development of the national market system.‚Äù[20] I will look for ways to bring us back in line with that intent and thereby promote innovation and competition in our markets. Finally, innovation and entrepreneurialism are the engines of the American economy. President Trump has described America as a ‚Äúnation of builders.‚Äù[21] Under my leadership, the Commission will encourage our nation‚Äôs builders rather than constrain them with red tape and one-size-fits-all rules. While the Commission is actively considering industry requests that could jumpstart innovative activity, we are also contemplating an innovation exemption that would allow registrants and non-registrants to quickly go to market with new business models and services that do not neatly fit within our existing rules and regulations. The Commission will continue to ensure that market participants adhere to certain conditions and requirements designed to achieve the policy aims of the federal securities laws. Under my vision for an innovation exemption, innovators and visionaries will be able to immediately enter the market with new technologies and business models but will not be required to comply with incompatible or burdensome prescriptive regulatory requirements that hinder productive economic activity. Instead, they will be able to comply with certain principles-based conditions designed to achieve the core policy aims of the federal securities laws. These conditions may include, for example, a commitment to make periodic reports to the Commission, incorporate whitelisting or ‚Äúverified pool‚Äù functionality, and restrict tokenized securities that do not adhere to a token standard that incorporates compliance features, such as ERC3643.[22] I encourage market participants and SEC staff alike to have an eye towards commercial viability when contemplating what various models could look like. *** As we advance these priorities, I look forward to working with my counterparts across the Administration to make the United States the crypto capital of the world. This represents more than a regulatory shift‚Äîit is a generational opportunity. From the leaves of a buttonwood tree to ledgers on a blockchain, the winds of innovation still blow‚Äîand it is our task that they carry American leadership forward. After all, ladies and gentlemen, we have never been content to follow. We will not watch from the sidelines. We will lead. We will build. And, we will ensure that the next chapter of financial innovation is written right here in America. Thank you very much for your time today. I encourage you to be attentive to our coming announcements and proposals and, as always, I welcome your thoughtful comments and suggestions. Title: Circle: The Digital Dollar‚Äôs Unlikely King
URL: https://www.forbes.com/sites/roomykhan/2025/07/31/circle-the-digital-dollars-unlikely-king/
Time Published: 2025-07-31T17:06:57Z
Full Content:
ByRoomy Khan ByRoomy Khan, Contributor. The Infrastructure Powerhouse Built on Regulation, Compliance, and Transparency. Why the Real Battle Is Just Beginning The numbers told the story before anyone understood it. Circle's IPO wasn't just big; it was a spectacle. Priced at $35, the stock rocketed to nearly $300 within weeks, setting off a Wall Street frenzy. USDC, the world's second-largest stablecoin issued by Circle, is used wherever value moves in crypto. It is embedded across protocols, blockchains, and DeFi platforms, and is becoming the connective layer between crypto and traditional finance. Circle stands as a rare asset in modern markets: a compliant, regulated, revenue-generating gateway to a financial system being rewritten in real time. Beneath the euphoria lies a paradox: Circle earns less from USDC than the partners helping distribute it. In 2024, Coinbase received $908 million, about 54 percent of Circle's total revenue. Circle retained the remaining 46 percent. That imbalance, as one-sided as it now seems, may have been the necessary price of market share. The revenue-sharing agreements weren't a misstep. They were Circle's way in: the cost of catching up, the price of market share. Circle launched USDC in 2018, entering a market where Tether, the largest stablecoin issuer, already had a four-year lead and dominated liquidity, adoption, and trading pairs across exchanges. Tether operated under an offshore model and looser compliance standards. Coming from behind, Circle played a different game, turning regulation into a competitive edge and transparency into a trust-building tool for regulators and institutions. The results validated the approach: between 2018 and 2021, USDC's supply surged more than 8,300 percent, nearly doubling Tether's growth over the same period. But there's a deeper risk: its revenue model, anchored in interest income, could unravel if rates come down materially. So why are stablecoins such a big deal? In the first half of 2025 alone, stablecoins processed $8.5 trillion in transactions. They're used in 89 percent of digital asset trades, power nearly half the liquidity in DeFi protocols managing over $200 billion, and now account for 15 percent of global retail cross-border payments. When someone buys Bitcoin on Coinbase, they're trading stablecoins, not dollars. Stablecoins slash cross-border payment costs by as much as 80 percent compared to traditional bank wires. Across Argentina, Vietnam, Turkey, and Nigeria, millions are turning to USDC for everything from groceries to rent, according to Chainalysis, which ranked these countries among the top five globally for grassroots crypto adoption in 2024. As stablecoins move into the mainstream, Circle's USDC is emerging as the default bridge between traditional finance and the blockchain economy. It's more than digital cash; it's an infrastructure layer for instant, 24/7 financial settlement. USDC spans 23 blockchains, is accepted by 89 percent of major exchanges, and reaches 500 million users across more than 180 countries through wallets and apps. In 2024 alone, its circulation jumped 78 percent to $64 billion, capturing a quarter of the global stablecoin market. Since launch, it has processed over $20 trillion in transactions, bridged $850 billion between crypto and traditional finance, and moved $20 billion through Circle's Cross‚ÄëChain Transfer Protocol. More importantly, USDC has proven its resilience under extreme stress. It survived the 2021‚Äì22 crypto winter when multiple stablecoins collapsed, maintained its peg during the Silicon Valley Bank crisis that threatened its reserve backing, and continued operating seamlessly while competitors faced regulatory scrutiny. Jamie Dimon, Wall Street's most influential banking CEO and longtime crypto skeptic, publicly acknowledged stablecoins in JPMorgan's Q2 earnings call: "We're going to be involved in both JPMorgan Depositcoin and stablecoins to understand it, to be good at it‚Ä¶ The way to be cognizant is to be involved." And no stablecoin is more institutionally aligned than USDC. Circle's compliance-first strategy looks less like a hedge and more like a masterstroke. Circle's business model is deceptively simple. It earns interest on the dollars backing every USDC in circulation. With more than $60 billion worth of tokens issued, Circle holds an equal amount in reserves. About 85 percent of those reserves sit in a BlackRock-managed money market fund earning around 4 percent annually; the rest is held in cash at major banks. That interest income makes up the bulk of Circle's revenue. But the company doesn't get to keep most of it. To grow USDC's footprint, Circle signed revenue-sharing agreements with major crypto exchanges. These deals gave its partners a share of reserve income to promote and integrate USDC. The most favorable terms went to Coinbase: USDC's co-creator and largest distributor. Under the agreement, Coinbase keeps 100 percent of the interest on USDC held on its platform, which typically totals tens of billions of dollars, plus 50 percent of net reserve income from all other USDC in circulation after other partners take their share. The deal auto-renews through 2026 and could extend to 2029 if usage milestones are hit. In 2024, Coinbase earned more from USDC than Circle did. These terms made sense when market penetration was the goal‚Äîbut now they leave Circle boxed in. Binance and Bybit also negotiated their own revenue-sharing deals, each securing a mid-to-high single-digit percentage of reserve income. While these cuts are smaller than Coinbase's, they still reduce Circle's overall take. In total, Circle retains only about 40 to 50 percent of net reserve income, all while bearing the full operational, compliance, and regulatory burden. And with interest rates expected to decline, the overall revenue pool is likely to shrink, tightening the squeeze just as Circle tries to scale into public markets. Circle isn't standing still. It's building new revenue streams that leverage its infrastructure: transaction fees, blockchain partnerships, and financial products with 100 percent gross margins. Blockchain partnerships monetize USDC's presence across networks. As new blockchains compete for users, hosting USDC brings instant legitimacy. Circle selectively charges integration fees for premium partners, turning network expansion into revenue. One of Circle's most effective pivots is charging fees for moving USDC between different blockchain networks. Think of it like a toll for crossing bridges between digital financial systems. Once offered for free, Circle now charges roughly 0.03 percent per transfer. With $20 billion in flows, it's become one of their fastest-growing income sources. Circle is also expanding into higher-margin financial products. Its acquisition of USYC, a digital money market fund, generates steady fee income from institutional exposure to government securities. Unlike USDC, these products carry no revenue splits. While these new revenue streams are gaining traction, Circle's long-term play is far bolder: embed USDC deep into the foundation of global finance. General payments represent a $240 trillion market where USDC's instant settlement could eliminate days-long delays and costly intermediaries. Circle envisions USDC becoming the digital backbone for everyday commerce, capturing a share of transaction fees from a market far larger than crypto trading. Cross-border payments offer the clearest near-term opportunity. Traditional wire transfers can take days, incur high fees, and pass through multiple correspondent banks. USDC can settle these transfers instantly and reduce costs by up to 80 percent. Circle's Cross-Border Payments Network is in early development, but partnerships with incumbents like FIS suggest a serious push into this space. Most ambitiously, Circle is partnering with ICE, the parent company of the New York Stock Exchange, to pilot the use of USDC as collateral and settlement infrastructure for traditional financial instruments. If successful, this could dramatically accelerate settlement times, lower capital requirements for traders, and enhance market efficiency, positioning Circle to earn fees on trillions in global equity, bond, and derivatives transactions. These early initiatives show how Circle could turn its compliance-driven infrastructure into a scalable, high-margin business. Then came the regulatory watershed moment. In July 2025, the GENIUS Act swept through Congress, mandating full reserves and strict licensing for all stablecoins. Competitors scrambled to comply. Circle? It was already there‚Äîwith monthly attestations, money transmitter licenses in 46 U.S. states, and years of regulatory groundwork. Overnight, its compliance burden became everyone else's nightmare‚Äîand Circle's competitive moat. Anyone can issue a stablecoin, but building trust, liquidity, and ubiquity takes years. Circle didn't just launch a token; it built the infrastructure everyone else now relies on. PayPal's PYUSD highlights the limits of closed ecosystems. Despite a massive user base, adoption has lagged due to limited utility outside its own platform. In contrast, adoption by Standard Chartered, Stripe, FIS, Visa, Mastercard, and even BlackRock reflects Circle's growing momentum. Near-zero interest rates would devastate Circle's revenue model, cutting reserve income while it surrenders 60 percent to partners. Regulatory winds could shift: a new administration might view private stablecoins as threatening dollar supremacy, or the Fed could launch its own digital currency. Most critically, if traditional finance adoption stalls, Circle's vision of embedding into global finance could remain just that: a vision. Without massive scale in traditional markets, its new revenue streams would stay subscale, leaving Circle dependent on a reserve interest model where it keeps the minority share. Now Circle is chasing the final piece of institutional legitimacy: a U.S. banking license. Its application for an FDIC charter is more than a regulatory formality; it's a strategic move to embed itself within the financial system. If approved, Circle would gain direct access to Fed payment rails, the ability to offer traditional banking services, and the trust halo of being a federally supervised institution. More importantly, it could position USDC as the safest digital dollar in circulation: government-regulated, fully reserved, and interoperable, without being state-controlled. In many ways, Circle functions like the Fed's unpaid R&D lab. Each USDC transaction is a real-time experiment in how programmable dollars might move through a modern financial system. Rather than launch a controversial FedCoin, regulators may opt to shape USDC, controlling the plumbing without owning the faucet. The application process won't be easy. Most crypto banking efforts have struggled to cross the finish line, and approval could take 18 to 24 months. But if Circle succeeds, it won't just sit on top of the financial system; it will become part of it. Circle's punishing revenue splits may reflect the cost of building foundational infrastructure. But if it executes on its trillion-dollar vision, those trade-offs could become footnotes to a much bigger success story.
