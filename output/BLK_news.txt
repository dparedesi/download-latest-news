The alliance‚Äôs spending surge will prompt war, cut services and worsen the climate crisis. NATO‚Äôs leaders agreed this week to invest 5 percent of their countries‚Äô gross domestic product (GDP) on ‚Äúcore defence requirements as well as defence and security-related spending by 2035‚Äù. NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte called it a ‚Äúquantum leap‚Äù in spending that would guarantee ‚Äúfreedom and security‚Äù for the military alliance‚Äôs one billion people. The headline demand for 5 percent GDP spending has been so loud, it‚Äôs easy to forget that for a long time, many NATO members considered the previous 2 percent goal either unachievable or unimportant. NATO first committed to its 2 percent GDP goal in 2002, but by 2021, only six of its members had achieved it. Yet three years later, 23 members had met the goal and all 32 are expected to comply by the end of 2025. This week, NATO has committed to more than doubling its spending to 5 percent of GDP. This will be partly met through creative accounting and reflects a desire to trumpet a big number to satisfy a petulant President Trump. The 5 percent headline includes 1.5 percent spent on military-related infrastructure, which could be broadly defined to include civilian expenditure. Even so, it reflects a huge escalation of military expenditure over the next decade from an already very high level. Last year, NATO spent $1.5 trillion on the military ‚Äì more than half of global military spending. If members comply with the core 3.5 percent target by 2030, that would mean a total of $13.4 trillion in military expenditure. It‚Äôs an impossible figure to grasp, but if you stacked it in one-dollar bills, you could make almost four piles that reach the moon. Belgium, the Netherlands and France followed suit, announcing aid cuts of 25 to 37 percent. The United States, under Trump, has decimated its overseas aid and climate programmes and reduced healthcare funding while proposing a record $1 trillion expenditure on the Pentagon. Europe is falling far behind on its own environmental and social goals, with its primary funding vehicle, the Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF), expiring in 2026. The European Trade Union Confederation (ETUC) concludes that most European NATO members will be unable to meet the 3.5 percent NATO target without cutting budgets, raising taxes or changing fiscal rules. NATO‚Äôs spending spree will not only divert money ‚Äì it will worsen the climate crisis. As one of the world‚Äôs biggest carbon polluters, it is investing in more gas-guzzling jets, tanks and missiles. Military emissions are notoriously hard to track due to limited data, but one report estimates that 3.5 percent of GDP spending would lead to 2,330 million metric tonnes of greenhouse gases by 2030 ‚Äì roughly the same as the combined annual emissions of Brazil and Japan. NATO‚Äôs justification is that increased investment is needed to confront the threats of ‚ÄúRussia‚Äù and ‚Äúterrorism‚Äù. Yet there is no rationale behind the 5 percent target or details on why threats to NATO have so drastically increased. Nor is there self-examination on how NATO‚Äôs actions partly set the stage for Russia‚Äôs invasion of Ukraine. Russia has increased military spending, but it still spends 10 times less than NATO. Nor could it catch up militarily with NATO‚Äôs 32-strong alliance, given its economy: $2 trillion in 2024 (nominal GDP), compared with $26 trillion for non-US NATO countries and $29 trillion for the US alone. NATO‚Äôs idea of security diverts money from social needs, worsens the climate crisis, rewards arms firms profiting from global conflict, and chooses war over diplomacy. Its bellicose stance in The Hague this week makes it one of the greatest threats to global security ‚Äì even to life on this planet. It is up to the peoples of NATO countries to reject this deadly path and reclaim security based on cooperation, justice and peace.
