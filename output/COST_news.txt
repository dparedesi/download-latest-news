Thirty-eight glimpses of a day 250 years in the making. So what is America? Do you know? Do any of us know anymore? We’re hermetically sealed in our information bubbles, sorted by our social-media feeds, feverishly working alone in our home offices, picking up essentials with a swipe and a tap rather than a trip to the store. You’ve heard a million times that we’re polarized, but this is something else: We’re atomized. Absent from one another. Us, here in this place that puts a lot of stock in a very important idea about collectivism: “Out of many, one.” (Perhaps you know the Latin: E pluribus unum.) If you haven’t heard, America’s got kind of a big birthday coming up. The lady turns 250 this year, and that means it’s high time to consider how she’s doing. To consider what she is. To consider who we are. It’s a tall order, trying to get your arms around a country the size of America, and maybe that’s why it’s been so easy for so many of us to slip into an existence in which we’ve lost touch with one another. Luckily, there’s a very simple way to halt the slide. It’s kind of the whole point of democracy, but we often fail to heed it outside of the formality of voting: Our civic duty is to listen to one another. If you want to know what America is, well—listen to Americans. Not so easy, of course; there are almost 350 million of us. And we’re pretty much all loud. But Esquire decided to get as close as a magazine can. On a single Friday in April, we carried pen and camera from sea to shining sea, visiting with Americans as far apart as a Hollywood scion and the diner waitress in the small town in Maine where the sun first hits the country. We covered thirty-three cities, both states outside the lower forty-eight, D.C., and Puerto Rico. We went fishing, we went farming, we flew, we partied, we ate Italian beef. So what is America? Many things. But it’s 2026, so let’s start here: It’s a gamble. I’m at a blackjack table when the woman next to me says she sees a bird. She’s forty or so, wearing a nice caftan and running shoes. She’s got her eyes up, hitching a golden shoulder bag across her chest, looking for the bird again. According to her, it’s careening among the crackling LED light of the slot machines surrounding us. “It’s called a phoebe,” she says, squinting. The dealer, trained to keep his eyes on the table, won’t look for the bird. “Birds happen,” he says. And me, I just can’t see it: bad eyes, too much hubbub, six hours of 3-6 Hold-’em with a half-kill, forty-three minutes of surprisingly lucky roulette, one bowl of crab soup, and three bets in the sportsbook. I was up eleven hundred bucks, which for me is what I term a nice circumstance there. And then blackjack at what turns out to be a pretty hot table. So yeah, we’re rolling near midnight in a desert casino outside Palm Springs, at the bottom of southern California. With a bird. Again and again, the woman gently drops two green chips onto the felt. Fifty bucks a hand. She’s got black chips to spare. “I’m a bird-watcher,” she says. “For serious, that’s a black phoebe.” What do I know? But the dealer has five. She doubles down on a nine and wins. I split my eights and win twice. It’s all good. But before another card falls, three frat-tastic college knobs jam up to the table, breathing all over the whole setup. Goodbye, hot table. The smallest one, with his Red Sox hat on backward, asks the dealer if they can bet seventy-eight dollars on a hand. It’s the last of their chips, he tells the world, and they want one last stab. Imagine. We’re in the middle of a shoe here, and I want to protest. But I notice another of the knobs has car keys in his hand, and all of a sudden I know what time it is. Yeah, yeah. No clocks in a casino. Everybody knows. But I can always sense the end of day from my seat at a gaming table. It comes to me in small signals: A guy glances at his watch while losing at roulette. A wandering girl lays her head on the shoulder of a bestie as they pass. Bearded baseball-hat guys step away from a losing table, shooting a glance back at the dealer whisking away their last palmful of chips. And guys like these guys, clenching car keys while betting one more time. So midnight has come, and everything in the once jolly churn of the casino looks a little kite-strung and stressed by the entirety of the day prior. In this way, the brokedown palace of dreams makes itself known to me once more. I no longer like the vibe. I pick up my bet, tip the dealer, and say bye to the woman. It’s over. Nothing lasts in a casino, but everything keeps going all the same. I cash out with $1,300, a nice circumstance there. Yeah, I’ll be back. Why not? It’s a new day. —Tom Chiarella Smalls is a basement club, but it feels more like a catacomb.
