Title: Navigating the rise of finfluencers: a multidimensional quality framework for strategic partnerships
URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1057/s41264-025-00334-7
Time Published: 2026-03-04T16:15:37Z
Full Content:
In the film Wall Street: Money Never Sleeps (2010), the antihero Gordon Gekko reflects on the moral ambiguity of modern financial services, stating: “(.) the beauty of the deal [taking other people’s money]: no one is responsible”. This phrase is gaining new relevance as financial influencers (finfluencers) are increasingly filling the financial advice gap by operating on various social platforms within a legal gray zone and with limited accountability (Barrett 2022; Moss et al. 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023). Even though there is no common definition yet, we follow Mölders et al. (2025), who define finfluencers as “personal brands who build relationships with an engaged audience through a regular flow of consistent, authentic and distinctive content posted on at least one social media platform that relates to, but is not limited to, personal finance and investment tips.”Footnote 1 While finfluencers can be seen as a distinct subset of expert-oriented and utilitarian influencers, their approaches diverge significantly: some position themselves as expert voices, drawing on formal qualifications, licenses, or professional experience to establish authority, while others adopt a utilitarian role, providing simplified, accessible financial tips for lay audiences (Mölders et al. 2025).Footnote 2 This dual positioning highlights the tension between perceived expertise and actual credentials, as well as the differing levels of accountability tied to each role. Comparable distinctions have been noted in the wider influencer marketing literature (e.g., Campbell and Farrell 2020; Vrontis et al. 2021; Han and Balabanis 2024) and are similarly relevant for other regulated domains, including medicine and politics (e.g., Ding et al. 2023). The rise of finfluencers was fueled by a number of factors, including the continuous expansion of global internet and social media usage (We Are Social et al. 2025), the pandemic (Boyde 2023; Davies 2023; Fava 2023), the growing valuation of global influencer marketing exceeding $30 billion in 2025 (Statista 2025), and the rising demand for financial literacy and advice (Calcagno and Monticone 2015; Barrett 2022). While the investment volume driven by finfluencers is difficult to pinpoint, finfluencers are currently outperforming their peers in other influencer niches, with double the follower growth on platforms like TikTok and Instagram (Emplifi 2024). Indeed, they seem to have a significant impact on the financial decision-making of younger generations as recent surveys show: e.g., in the United Kingdom (UK), over 44% of Gen Z state using information from social media for investment decisions; in the United States (US), 37% of Gen Z say finfluencers play a major role in their investment decisions (FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023); and in Germany, more than half of Gen Z and Millennials view social media as an alternative to traditional financial advice (German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority [BaFin] 2024). However, finfluencers tend to concentrate on high-risk asset classes such as cryptocurrencies, where social media hype and fear of missing out (FOMO) often drive investment decisions (Yamamoto et al. 2020; FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023; Merkley et al. 2024). A similar phenomenon has been observed in NFTs (non-fungible tokens), where pricing and trading volumes are closely tied to finfluencer activity rather than fundamentals (e.g., Kapoor et al. 2022; Horky et al. 2023). Thus, finfluencers can also be seen as facilitators of speculative retail investing (Mölders et al. 2025) and the crypto ecosystem. This is consistent with headlines such as “Eight Twitter finance gurus charged with criminal pump-and-dump scheme” (Robertsen 2022), which highlight the financial risks associated with “finfluencing charlatans” (adapted from Berk and Binsbergen 2022). In this prominent case, also known as Atlas Trading, eight Twitter-based finfluencers were indicted by US authorities for stock manipulation and defrauding retail investors of over $100 million (Securities and Exchange Commission [SEC] 2022b; US Department of Justice 2022). Beyond this, there have been numerous cases of fraudulent promotion of financial services (also by celebrities) and “get rich quick” investment schemes via social media (e.g., Lukpat 2022; Michaels 2022; Davies 2023). In response to such significant financial losses and consumer harm, regulators and authorities around the globe are turning their eyes on finfluencers (e.g., Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets [AFM] 2021a); Australian Securities and Investments Commission 2022; BaFin 2022; SEC 2022a, 2022b; European Securities and Markets Authority 2023; Financial Conduct Authority 2023a, 2023b, 2023c; Securities and Exchange Board of India 2023). For instance, the German regulatory body has issued the following warning: “On social media, you will find real experts – but many self-proclaimed experts as well. Both types are also to be found among the financial influencers (known as ‘finfluencers’)” (BaFin 2022). It is therefore not surprising that current research predominately discusses consumer harm and protection and corresponding legal perspectives (e.g., Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Hall 2023; Khurana 2023; Ramaswamy 2023; Stefanou 2023; Guan 2023, 2024). Apart from the consumer protection perspective, finfluencers could also be integrated as strategic business partners (Enke and Borchers 2019; Borchers and Enke 2021). This is especially relevant for financial services marketing (e.g., Mensah and Amenuvor 2022; Ho and Amin 2023; Nguyen et al. 2024). However, businesses seeking strategic partnerships with finfluencers are at risk of damaging their own reputation by potentially collaborating with finfluencing charlatans (e.g., Campbell and Farrell 2020; McMullan 2023). In consequence, we perceive a gap in the current academic discourse regarding the quality of finfluencers, which has been addressed only one-dimensionally based on their investing performance (Kakhbod et al. 2023; Espeute and Preece 2024). In response, we address this gap and follow calls for further research on new technologies, especially social media and finfluencers (Bhaskar et al. 2023; Hudders and Lou 2023; Bapat and Vashisht 2024; Yeo et al. 2024) and provide a complementary solution to economically costly over-regulation (Berk and Binsbergen 2022). Thus, our study aims to derive a multidimensional finfluencer quality (FinQ) framework to guide finfluencers’ stakeholders to select high-quality strategic business partners. Hence, we formulate our guiding research question (RQ) as follows: RQ What are quality indicators of trustworthy finfluencers and how can these indicators be systematically integrated into a multidimensional quality framework for facilitating strategic partnerships? We applied a mixed-method approach to answer the RQ. In the first step, we conceptualized a preliminary FinQ framework based on existing theory. In a second step, we used 12 expert interviews to refine and complement the preliminary FinQ framework, which resulted in the identification of 40 quality indicators (N = 40). These quality indicators can be categorized into five quality dimensions (QDs): (QD1) Compliance, (QD2) Content and Communication, (QD3) Credibility, (QD4) Profile Structure and (QD5) Engagement. In a last step, the refined FinQ framework was validated in terms of practical applicability and operationalization by a subsequent multiple-case study selecting five German-speaking finfluencers on Instagram. While our study tackles quality uncertainty surrounding finfluencers, it also encourages businesses to form strategic partnerships with credible, high-quality finfluencers, particularly in financial services marketing. The FinQ framework supports the professionalization of such partnerships by enabling more informed selection decisions. While it cannot fully eliminate charlatans, it helps financial service providers identify trustworthy partners, avoid reputational risks, and raise standards over time. Beyond financial services, the framework can also inform regulatory efforts, consumer protection, and strategic collaborations in other areas, such as listed firms’ financial communication or governmental financial literacy campaigns. The business model of finfluencers involves referral and reward networks potentially leading to conflicts of interest (Campbell and Farrell 2020; Reingen and Kernan 1986; Verlegh et al. 2013). Such networks usually compensate finfluencers on a commission basis upon referral of a client for a specific financial service or product (e.g., by using affiliate links or promo codes) (AFM 2021b). Thus, we assume that finfluencers face moral hazard because of their conflicted business model (Arrow 1985; Alon et al. 2021). In this context, when the lemon principle, referring to the problem of quality uncertainty in markets with information asymmetry between buyers and sellers (Akerlof 1970), is applied to finfluencers (sellers), their stakeholders (buyers; here consumers or followers and businesses seeking partnerships) face quality uncertainty, wherein we assume finfluencers are better informed. As a solution, Leland (1979) found that minimum quality constraints or licensing requirements tackle quality uncertainty in high-skill professions (e.g., lawyers or doctors). This can be applied to finfluencers, where typically rather uninformed followers consume conflicted financial (unregulated) advice. However, regulation of finfluencers may also reduce competition in the financial advisory market, which is why regulating finfluencers is not always the best option for consumer welfare depending on the level of consumer harm (Berk and Binsbergen 2022). However, there is currently no literature that empirically assesses the regulation of finfluencers from an economic perspective. While moral hazard provides a useful lens to study commission-driven finfluencer activity, not all creators of financial content on social media operate primarily for personal profit. Many also share content to educate, foster communities, build their personal brand, or even for entertainment (Guan 2023; Mölders et al. 2025). This heterogeneity of finfluencers tempers the reach of the moral hazard argument but still reinforces the need for a quality framework that applies across diverse motivation profiles. FOMO has been identified as a key driver of financial decision-making of Gen Z (FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023), which puts emphasis on how moral hazard stems not only from conflicted incentives on the supply side but also from emotional manipulation on the demand or consumer side. While FOMO is conceptualized as a utility-based reaction to perceived opportunity loss from an economic perspective (Kaddouhah 2024), it can distort rational financial decision-making. Recent empirical studies show that such appeals amplify impulsive, risky and short-term oriented financial decision-making (Friederich et al. 2024; Meyer et al. 2024; Symbiosis and Gandhi 2024; Mölders et al. 2025) and reinforce herding dynamics and overreliance on social heuristics leading to systematic underperformance (Kakhbod et al. 2023; Gerritsen and Regt 2025; Haase et al. 2025). These findings highlight the need to address not only information asymmetries on the supply side, but also the risk of behavioral biases on the consumer side when assessing FinQ. Ultimately, we aim to conceptualize a FinQ framework that helps (corporate) stakeholders navigate the largely unregulated space of financial advice delivered by finfluencers on social media platforms. In markets with information asymmetries, where the true quality of a provider is difficult to assess, such frameworks can reduce uncertainty and support informed decision-making (Stiglitz 2002). Building on this idea, a FinQ framework enabling benchmarking (Cook and Luo 2003; Aiken et al. 2004; Hörner and Lambert 2021) may not only incentivize finfluencers to meet defined quality standards but also foster transparency and comparability. As a result, it might also encourage healthy competition and introduce a form of peer pressure and accountability within the finfluencer market (Calvó-Armengol and Jackson 2010). Quality is described by the international quality management standard ISO 9000:2015 as the “degree to which a set of inherent characteristics of (…) [a service or product] fulfils requirements.” Following this customer-oriented definition, we need to identify quality requirements (or standards) to establish a quality framework for finfluencers. The role of such a “standard setter” can be taken on by different stakeholders including researchers (Soni Raleigh and Foot 2010). In a first step, to measure quality, we must define a set of quality indicators. Therefore, we employ the concept of key performance indicators (KPIs).Footnote 3 Therefore, measuring quality depends on meeting defined KPIs as focal units of the framework.Footnote 4 In a second step, such defined KPIs will be aggregated into quality dimensions (QDs), which will serve as the conceptual cornerstones of the preliminary FinQ framework. This approach is consistent with the grouping of performance metrics into strategic dimensions, as seen in frameworks such as the Balanced Scorecard according to Kaplan and Norton (1992). In addition to considering moral hazard and behavioral biases, FinQ assessment needs to account for an environment of regulatory ambiguity, where jurisdictional inconsistencies and limited enforcement capacity can enable non-compliant or even unlawful practices and emerging forms of financial misconduct (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Stefanou 2023; International Organization of Securities Commissions 2025). We acknowledge that compliance under conditions of regulatory ambiguity often operates in a gray zone shaped by evolving standards, limited enforcement, and a strong reliance on self-regulation. These broader structural challenges serve as contextual boundary conditions that reinforce the urgency of establishing transparent and stakeholder-oriented quality criteria. Against this backdrop, we introduce five QDs grounded in the literature that serve as guiding pillars for structuring the FinQ framework: First, compliance with regulation is an inherent prerequisite for quality and gatekeeping dimension for influencer selection, especially in the light of corporate strategic partnerships with finfluencers (Espeute and Preece 2024; Mölders et al. 2025). However, the extent of compliance is dependent on the context, with requirements varying according to the country and the applicable regulation, which often remain fragmented and evolving (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Pflücke 2023). In the development of the FinQ framework, we based our approach on the German and European regulatory framework (see Appendix A). However, such regulatory and legal framework as a basis for compliance requirements must be subject to review and adaptation in each country or market. Consequently, we established QD1 Compliance. Second, we observe that most qualitative KPIs tend to be holistically defined in the existing literature, which is possibly due to their rather limited accessibility and measurability. Thus, we were able to identify rather holistic KPIs such as “post quality” (content quality) and “professional communication” from existing literature (Gräve and Greff 2018; Gräve 2019). However, such KPIs need to be further broken down and refined to be measured accurately. Other connected more specific KPIs refer for example to “playfulness”, which refers to the level of entertainment (Kim and Baek 2022). In response to such holistic content-related and communicative KPIs we formulate QD2 Content and Communication. Third, we also find that “experience” (but also track record) and “expertise” is often associated with influencer quality (e.g., Gräve and Greff 2018; Kim and Baek 2022). This is complemented by KPIs such as “trustworthiness”, “authenticity” or even “attractiveness” of influencers (e.g., Chekima et al. 2020; Primasiwi et al. 2021; Wiedmann and Mettenheim 2021). We refer to attractiveness on a personal level, which has been shown to enhance perceived trustworthiness and expertise through halo effects (Ohanian 1990; Reingen and Kernan 1993). In this context, credibility emerges as a central concept in the literature (e.g., Belanche et al. 2021; Bi and Zhang 2023; Dhun and Dangi 2023). To address the perceived importance of the credibility of finfluencers, we accordingly specify the principal dimension QD3 Credibility. Lastly, influencer marketing practitioners mostly rely on well-established and easily accessible quantitative KPIs such as community size, engagement rate, target group fit, posting frequency, sentiment or impressions (Gräve and Greff 2018; Gräve 2019; Primasiwi et al. 2021). This is also consistent with major influencer audit or due diligence platforms such as HypeAuditor and Upfluence (Alain 2022; Komok 2024). Thus, we derive QD4 Profile Structure and QD5 Engagement. While QD4 assesses how effectively a finfluencer manages their content and cross-platform presence, QD5 focuses on quantitative engagement metrics such as community size and interactions. However, studies by Goldenberg et al. (2024) and Hinz et al. (2011) emphasize that reach alone is insufficient, as true influence depends on content–audience fit, credibility, and network dynamics. This is supported by Lanz et al. (2019) who show that mid-tier influencers can outperform macro-influencers in engagement and trust.Footnote 5 Existing influencer quality frameworks in marketing provide useful foundations for evaluating influencer effectiveness. Leung et al. (2022) highlight influencer selection as a key step, focusing on traits like network size, engagement, authenticity, and brand fit (Gräve and Greff 2018; Lou and Yuan 2019; see also Audrezet et al. 2020). While the FinQ framework reflects general influencer criteria via QD4–5, existing frameworks often overlook finance-specific factors, especially compliance, regulatory risk, and the pitfalls of popularity-driven metrics. FinQ addresses this gap by embedding domain-specific requirements, aligning quality assessment with the unique expectations of financial services. It is built on five core dimensions and over 15 KPIs, which are refined and validated in the following steps (see Table 1; Fig. 1). Conceptual framework Our research is intended to further refine and validate the conceptualized preliminary FinQ framework derived from existing literature. In response to the novelty of the finfluencer phenomenon, we follow an exploratory sequential mixed-method approach according to Creswell and Creswell (2023) for the framework development (see Fig. 2). Therefore, we refined the preliminary FinQ framework through in-depth qualitative analysis of expert interviews, followed by a validation for practical applicability and robustness through a multiple-case study following Dehghani et al. (2022). Research framework Therefore, in the first step, we discussed the preliminary FinQ framework with an expert panel to identify the necessary expertise, partially following the approach proposed by Negrin et al. (2022) and the guidance on qualitative interviewing of Edwards and Holland (2015). Accordingly, an interview guideline (see Appendix B) was derived, which was followed by subsequent pilot interviews (Majid et al. 2017). After a feedback loop, further participants were recruited through online research and direct referrals (Noy 2008). The recruitment process was continued until the point at which theoretical saturation was increasingly evident, as indicated by the level of relevance in Table 2 and the corresponding reduced number of new code categories (quality indicators or KPIs) added sequentially by new interview participants as shown in Table 4 (Hennink et al. 2017). We interviewed various participants, including but not limited to financial journalists, financial service providers, consumer advocates, and finfluencers, capturing multiple perspectives on FinQ to ensure stakeholder triangulation (see column “Stakeholder type” in Table 2; Campbell et al. 2020). The semi-structured expert interviews (59.5 min on average; 12 h in total) were conducted from November 2023 to January 2024. All interviews were held online and recorded using Zoom after verbal and tool-based consent. After recording, the interviews were transcribed using Sonix.ai and subsequently coded and analyzed using the qualitative data analysis software MAXQDA. While fundamentally following the grounded theory of Corbin and Strauss (2015), the focus of this research was to expand the identified quality indicators (KPIs) and refine the preliminary quality framework. In this context, we applied a hybrid deductive–inductive coding approach. The five QDs, derived from literature, were used as top-level thematic categories, while the specific KPIs emerged inductively from the data. Interview transcripts were thematically coded in MAXQDA, with potential quality indicators grouped under the corresponding QD through iterative refinement. Coding was led by one researcher and cross-checked by a second to ensure consistency and interpretive reliability. To further enhance robustness, the built-in generative AI of MAXQDA was used to support the refinement process (Morgan 2023; Zhang et al. 2023). In the subsequent validation, a multiple-case study of individual Instagram accounts (Vaus 2001; Yin 2018) tested the practical applicability of the conceptually derived FinQ framework.Footnote 6 A market benchmark from Zülch et al. (2024) was used to select and benchmark five German-speaking finfluencers with data as of January 2024 (refer to Table 3). The cases were selected based on their market position and popularity, entailing a filtering process (with a minimum of 1,000 followers) and track record (operating on Instagram for at least one year), following the guidance of Miles and Huberman (1994). To ensure variation, cases were selected by gender and topical focus to capture diverse finfluencer types and content strategies. Ten posts per candidate (including pinned and random) were independently assessed in a double-blind review, with discrepancies averaged after a pilot for pretesting and calibration. All expert interviews were conducted in German, and translated quotes are provided throughout the analysis to illustrate key findings (the original German quotations are available upon request). The refinement of the preliminary FinQ framework resulted in a comprehensive set of 40 KPIs (N = 40) within the five previously established QDs. Notably, the most frequently coded KPIs are: (QD1) advertising disclosure and licenses/permission, (QD2) accuracy/correctness, (QD3) expertise/qualification, independence/neutrality and reliability/social proof, (QD4) target group fit and (QD5) community size and reach. In the following, we will use chosen citations of the expert interviews to not only dive deeper into the qualitative data set, but also to support the refined FinQ framework (see Table 4). QD1 Compliance is seen as “(…) the highest priority, it is always the first starting point (…).” (Interviewee 2). Thus, adherence to “Compliance is (…) conditio sine qua non” (Interviewee 4) making it a fundamentally non-negotiable QD with a total of eight KPIs. Consequently, regarding strategic partnerships in the financial services industry, this means managers and practitioners should have “a basic fear, which is reputation at risk” (Interviewee 4). It should be ensured that there is no promotional collaboration with finfluencers who do “not [ensure] one hundred per cent compliance” (Interviewee 4). In response, Compliance therefore follows the “all-or-nothing principle” as gatekeeping dimension. Furthermore, “transparent disclosure of funding” (Interviewee 6) is relevant to avoid conflicts of interest and signal potential moral hazard to followers. In this context, transparency is a high priority regarding advertising disclosure: “if you get money from someone, make it transparent” (Interviewee 7). QD2 Content and Communication encompasses nine exclusively qualitative KPIs, which tends to be a more subjective QD. However, the experts consider “criteria such as variety, ease of understanding and perhaps entertainment value” (Interviewee 3) to be particularly relevant. On the one hand, this dimension primarily concentrates on “the quality of the content, i.e. how high is the quality of the content produced?” (Interviewee 8). On the other hand, the focus is also on “professionalism in communication, (…) [i.e.] how do I communicate with followers, (…) even also if I present myself elsewhere?” (Interviewee 5). Managers and practitioners should therefore pay particular attention to the professionalism of finfluencers when engaging in strategic partnerships. Finally, objectivity is highlighted as an extremely important quality indicator within this QD, even though the business model seems to be conflicted: “One of the key factors is neutrality, incorruptibility [of finfluencers]” (Interviewee 10). QD3 Credibility is seen as another critical QD: “But credibility is, I think, (…) the most important thing.” (Interviewee 10). Following the breakdown and refinement of the KPIs identified in the literature review, a total of nine KPIs were identified for this QD. The experts consider “the personal experience (..) [finfluencers] have [as] very, very important.” (Interviewee 2). In addition, “relevant prior education or self-taught through a track record” (Interviewee 12) are relevant for the experts to assess the credibility of finfluencers. In consequence, “the track record (…) and the (…) expertise [play] a very important role” (Interviewee 8). These KPIs should also be considered by managers and practitioners when screening finfluencers to engage in strategic partnerships. Notably, the experts also stress that “channels where you don’t have a face” (Interviewee 11) or channels “that are based (…) [in a third country] and hide behind masks or pictures” (Interviewee 5) are early warning signs not to engage in strategic partnerships because of reputational risks of potential charlatans. QD4–5 Profile Structure and Engagement mostly cover well-established quantitative KPIs (14 in total), where especially community size and engagement rate are seen as particularly relevant for strategic partnerships: But “We don’t just want to have (…) influencers who have a high reach, we also need good followers who have a high engagement rate” (Interviewee 12). However, some experts hinted not to overestimate QD4 and QD5: (1) as they mostly cover generally applicable influencer KPIs not specific to finfluencers, (2) popular finfluencers “take it all”, (3) dilution of relevance by bots and fake engagement, and (4) there is some overlapping and correlation with the other mostly qualitative QDs (as kind of “residual KPIs” as a consequence of QD1-3). In this context, for a large-scale analysis of high engagement rate outliers and their potential dilution through scams and fakes, especially on Instagram see, e.g., Narassiguin and Sargent (2019). Furthermore, for potential overlapping and correlation of quantitative “traditional” influencer KPIs, see Gräve (2019). We developed a FinQ scorecard based on the refined framework (see Appendix C), revealing diverse quality profiles and content strategies. While some finfluencers combined financial expertise with practical advice, others focused on lifestyle or niche audiences. One candidate was excluded for non-compliance, and another was lacking finance-relevant qualifications. Engagement rates ranged from 0.22% to 1.70%, placing our sample between the 3rd and 40th benchmark percentile, reflecting relatively low levels. This supports prior findings that larger audiences often correlate with weaker engagement, while micro and nano influencers tend to show stronger relative engagement (e.g., Kay et al. 2020; Conde and Casais 2023). Further, the validation demonstrates that the FinQ framework facilitates a differentiation between high-, medium-, and low-quality finfluencers, even though a larger-scale application would be required to establish a robust market benchmark. While individual dimensions (e.g., engagement only) alone provide limited insight, multidimensional integration of qualitative and quantitative indicators reveal distinct quality profiles. Therefore, high-quality finfluencers need to perform consistently well across all QDs, demonstrating strong compliance, expertise and credible communication, while lower-quality finfluencers tend to exhibit selective strengths (e.g. high engagement but weak credibility). This demonstrates the added value of the FinQ framework when it comes to distinguishing reliable strategic partners from those who are less credible, or even potential charlatans. In total, 28 of the original 40 KPIs from the refined FinQ framework were validated and operationalized for the scorecard used in the multiple-case study (see Table 4). To ensure empirical applicability, the selection emphasized practicability and measurability over exhaustive coverage. Specifically, (1) KPIs that were observable and assessable via publicly available social media content (e.g., posting frequency, platform consistency) were prioritized; and (2) KPIs that proved overly subjective, ambiguous, or infeasible for reliable cross-case assessment (e.g., sentiment, self-monitoring, or internal compliance structures) were excluded. Importantly, the removal of 12 KPIs does not imply diminished theoretical relevance; these indicators remain part of the broader conceptual framework but were excluded from scoring due to methodological constraints. A full list of excluded KPIs is provided in Appendix D. To accommodate the varied nature of the KPIs, the FinQ scorecard applies different scale types: binary checks for compliance thresholds, dichotomous ratings for “all-or-nothing” factors like formal qualification, Likert-type ratings for nuanced judgments by analysts, and benchmark-based scoring for quantitative metrics (e.g., engagement rates). This mix ensures rigor while allowing consistent cross-case comparison. We also acknowledge that engagement metrics (QD5) may be distorted by inorganic activity, such as bots or fake accounts, which are known to inflate financial discourse on platforms like Instagram (Narassiguin and Sargent 2019; Kraaijeveld and Smedt 2020). While our scorecard relied on publicly observable engagement data, future applications of the FinQ framework should incorporate bot audit tools and fraud detection to improve the robustness of engagement scoring. The validation demonstrates the FinQ framework’s practical utility and benchmarking potential. It allows companies to identify strategic partners offering a replicable and scalable foundation for finfluencer due diligence in financial services. To enhance practical applicability, we translated the validated dimensions into a concise due diligence checklist (Appendix E). This tool complements the scorecard by offering clear, actionable questions that support consistent, real-world assessments of finfluencers for managerial decision-making. We establish a multidimensional FinQ framework that extends current literature on finfluencers beyond calling for regulation (e.g., Pflücke 2022, 2023). While moral hazard and quality uncertainty persist (AFM 2021b; Arrow 1985; BaFin 2022), conflicted financial advice may still be preferable to none (Chalmers and Reuter 2020; Mölders et al. 2025). Accordingly, the FinQ framework offers a complementary approach to avoid costly over-regulation (Berk and Binsbergen 2022) by enabling stakeholders to distinguish trustworthy finfluencers from charlatans and promote informed consumer decision-making (Stiglitz 2002; Hörner and Lambert 2021). This approach can be reinforced through trust marks, peer pressure, and reward mechanisms that foster professionalization within the finfluencer domain (Cook and Luo 2003; Aiken et al. 2004; Calvó-Armengol and Jackson 2010). At the same time, social media platforms increasingly act as gatekeepers through algorithmic moderation and compliance enforcement (e.g., Schroeder 2025). Their incentive to tackle misleading financial content aligns with broader accountability and regulatory goals, and the FinQ framework could further inform automated “charlatan detection” or other platform-based screening approaches by outlining key dimensions for identifying low-quality or harmful financial content. In consequence, high-quality finfluencers can be powerful allies in promoting better investment behaviors by addressing under-confidence, financial education gaps and risk tolerance (Heo et al. 2021). Notably, they could also contribute to overcoming the barriers that consumers encounter when obtaining financial advice (Moss et al. 2022). This is enabled by audience segmentation based on cognitive styles, enhancing content effectiveness and long-term financial decision-making and literacy (Estelami and Estelami 2024). Ultimately, finfluencers might also impact consumer behavior, supporting the adoption of new technologies through both community-building and endorsements (Nguyen et al. 2024). Our FinQ framework promotes transparency and indicates quality, which is essential for the selection of trustworthy finfluencers for all kinds of strategic partnerships. Thus, our work supports stakeholders to identify relevant high-quality finfluencers with which to engage in partnerships, thereby reducing the risk of reputational damages from finfluencing charlatans (Singh et al. 2020; Hudders et al. 2021). Further, such stakeholders save internal resources and reduce their risk exposure through guiding their selection strategy of relevant finfluencers (Saima and Khan 2021; Ren et al. 2023; Venciute et al. 2023). To further increase managerial usability, we developed two complementary tools based on the validated framework: (1) a FinQ scorecard (see Appendix C) that enables systematic benchmarking (FinQ-Score), and (2) a due diligence checklist (see Appendix E) that translates the validated KPIs into practical binary (yes or no) questions. These tools empower managers to conduct efficient, transparent, and criteria-based finfluencer assessments. In consequence, we see three main stake-holding corporate beneficiaries: First, our work guides sales and marketing managers of financial services businesses to extend their social media engagement and to select relevant high-quality finfluencers as trusted voices and brand ambassadors to especially target the digitally savvy younger generations (Anand and Mishra 2022; Mensah and Amenuvor 2022; Ho and Amin 2023; Chikweche et al. 2024; Lom et al. 2024; Nguyen et al. 2024). Second, our work also supports publicly listed companies in selecting finfluencers for extending their financial communication strategies targeted at attracting younger retail investors to diversify their shareholder basis (Binder-Tietz et al. 2024; Mölders et al. 2025). Finally, our work offers insights to financial planners and advisers, enabling them to gain a deeper understanding of finfluencers as competitors that present a challenge to their current business model (Holger 2020; Tkachenko 2022; Fava 2023; Reiter et al. 2023). In addition to corporate beneficiaries, we find that our research could also support managers of regulatory bodies and policymakers to refine current regulatory practices and consumer protection even though we acknowledge that the FinQ framework can only complement rethinking the current regulation of finfluencers (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Guan 2023, 2024; Hall 2023; Stefanou 2023; Espeute and Preece 2024). Our exploratory research is inherently limited due to the dynamic regulatory and technological environment, but also particularly regarding the variable understanding of quality itself. Thus, future research should further differentiate how the FinQ framework handles various finfluencer types, as quality may mean different things for licensed advisors versus unqualified promoters of high-risk assets (potential charlatans). In addition, the FinQ scorecard itself offers the potential to profile such different types (e.g., highly professional, lifestyle-oriented, and entertainment-driven). Future studies could leverage these profiles to map the distribution of finfluencer types across markets and platforms, tracking how they evolve over time. Further, our research is limited by involving an in-depth but small qualitative sample. Also, while mixed-method approaches enhance triangulation of data, they can lead to biases (Campbell et al. 2020; Creswell and Creswell 2023). In consequence, we encourage future studies to employ larger-scale quantitative designs to validate, refine, and potentially generalize our findings, for instance, by testing the FinQ-Score against the actual performance of investment recommendations from a broader set of scored finfluencers. Furthermore, reliance on historical data may not accurately reflect status quo. Thus, further research could aim at keeping objectivity and up-to-dateness (e.g., using AI-powered solutions or follower involvement) and extend the FinQ framework into a longitudinal tool, enabling repeated assessments to capture shifts in reputation and content quality over time. In addition, platform differences present a challenge to applicability and validity of our multiple-case study highlighting the need for cross-platform research. Lastly, we were unable to include all KPIs in the refined FinQ framework due to feasibility constraints, lack of public assessability or observability, and the exclusion of overly subjective or redundant indicators. Moreover, our engagement scoring may not fully capture distortions from bots or fake accounts. Future applications of the FinQ framework should integrate bot-audit tools and fraud detection to enhance robustness. Additionally, our FinQ framework is based on German and European Union law, which might necessitate adjustments of the Compliance dimension for its application to other markets and countries. In response, we call for the development of “generally accepted quality standards for finfluencers” as an extension to our FinQ framework. Finally, future studies could evaluate the FinQ framework’s effectiveness by linking it to actual success metrics of strategic partnerships with finfluencers, such as lead generation, return on investment (ROI), or changes in brand trust. While promising, this step requires longitudinal or other designs that go beyond the scope of our study. Nonetheless, we highlight it as a critical future direction to assess the long-term impact and strategic value of high-quality finfluencer partnerships. Ultimately, other researchers may use our framework as groundwork and provide novel further insights into leveraging high-quality finfluencers as a distinctive opportunity for the financial services industry to cultivate trust, engage with diverse audiences, and develop tailored marketing strategies that resonate with younger consumers in the age of social media. Returning to the introductory quote of the antihero Gordon Gekko, finfluencers currently lack accountability, and their mostly commission-based business model involves moral hazard and behavioral biases which not only causes quality uncertainty, but also potential consumer harm and financial losses. In response, we acknowledge the need for reassessing their current regulation to protect consumers (e.g., through setting minimum quality constraints). However, we see a risk of overregulating finfluencers, which could be not only costly from an economic perspective, but also may take away the opportunity to integrate finfluencers as potential allies in corporate and governmental strategic partnerships. In response, our FinQ framework is a complementary approach to tackle the current lack of accountability. In this context, our findings could accelerate professionalization by enabling informed decision making to eventually drive finfluencing charlatans out of the market. Further, our research builds on improving trustworthiness as a basis for encouraging strategic partnerships with high-quality finfluencers. We also find that regulators and governmental bodies should uphold a direct dialogue with high-quality finfluencers to integrate them into policymaking and strategies on promoting financial education, inclusion and literacy. We find that finfluencers may prove to be more than a short-term phenomenon or a further source of noise within the capital markets. Rather, they may represent a long-term game changer for the digital financial community and the financial services industry. Title: Are mega-cap AI stocks actually worth what we’re paying for them
URL: https://techpinions.com/are-mega-cap-ai-stocks-actually-worth-what-were-paying-for-them/
Time Published: 2026-03-02T15:00:00Z
Full Content:
The Magnificent Seven will spend an estimated $680 billion on capital expenditure in 2026, most of it on AI infrastructure. Their combined market capitalization exceeds $16 trillion. Their aggregate earnings growth is expected to hit 22.8% this year. And yet the single most important question in public markets right now has nothing to do with whether these companies are growing — it’s whether any amount of growth can justify what investors are paying for them. The answer is more complicated than either the bulls or bears want to admit. Every market cycle produces a valuation debate, and this one is no different — except in scale. The five largest companies now account for 30% of the S&P 500 and 20% of the MSCI World index, the greatest concentration in half a century. The Case-Shiller price-to-earnings ratio for the US market has exceeded 40 for the first time since the dot-com crash. These aren’t numbers that invite complacency. But they’re also not the numbers that defined the late 1990s, and understanding the difference matters more than picking a side. Start with the fundamentals. The Magnificent Seven trade at roughly 28 times expected earnings — elevated, certainly, but roughly half the tech sector’s valuation during the dot-com era. NVIDIA trades at about 26 times forward earnings despite growing its earnings 130% in fiscal 2025, with another 56% growth expected in fiscal 2026. These aren’t profitless companies riding momentum. They’re cash-generating machines trading at premiums that, while uncomfortable, have historical precedent during transformational technology shifts. The bubble comparison falls apart on one critical dimension: profitability. In 2000, the median internet stock had negative earnings and no clear path to profitability. In 2026, the Magnificent Seven generate hundreds of billions in combined annual profit. Meta’s AI-driven ad targeting is delivering measurable revenue growth. Microsoft’s Azure AI services generated $51.5 billion in a single quarter. Amazon’s AWS remains the profit engine underwriting everything else the company does. The business case for enterprise AI is moving from theoretical to measurable in these companies’ actual financial results. Economist Robin Greenwood’s bubble-detection framework, which relies on four conditions — overvaluation, bubble beliefs, issuance, and inflows — finds only three of the four present in early 2026. The missing ingredient is issuance. Unlike the dot-com era, which produced an explosion of IPOs from companies with no revenue, the AI boom has been remarkably restrained on new issuance. That absence alone doesn’t make the market safe, but it does differentiate this cycle from history’s most destructive bubbles. Now for the uncomfortable part. The Magnificent Seven plan to spend $680 billion on capital expenditure this year, with the lion’s share going to AI data centers, chips, and infrastructure. Alphabet nearly doubled its capex guidance to $180 billion. Meta raised its 2026 target to between $115 billion and $135 billion. Amazon is guiding for $200 billion — up from $131 billion last year. Goldman Sachs projects total AI company investment will exceed $500 billion in 2026 alone. These numbers require generating roughly $2 trillion in annual AI revenue by 2030 to justify the expenditure. Current AI-specific revenues across the entire industry stand at approximately $20 billion. That’s a 100-fold gap that needs to close in four years. Even for companies with extraordinary execution track records, that trajectory demands a combination of enterprise adoption speed and consumer willingness to pay that hasn’t materialized yet. Only 5% of ChatGPT users currently pay for the service. Microsoft’s 15 million paying Copilot customers represent a fraction of its 450 million Microsoft 365 base. The capex-to-revenue ratios are reaching levels that would have been unthinkable for technology companies even five years ago. Hyperscalers now spend 45% to 57% of revenue on capital expenditure — ratios that resemble utility companies more than software businesses. Morgan Stanley estimates that hyperscalers will borrow around $400 billion in 2026, more than double the $165 billion borrowed in 2025. When Morningstar flags the AI spending spree as a potential source of trouble for investors, the concern isn’t hypothetical — it’s arithmetic. The most useful way to think about mega-cap tech valuations isn’t bull versus bear — it’s what the current prices implicitly assume. At 28 times forward earnings, the Magnificent Seven are pricing in roughly 20% to 25% earnings growth for the next several years. That’s not impossible, but it leaves almost no margin for error. Any deceleration in cloud revenue growth, any quarter where AI capex rises faster than AI revenue, any sign that enterprise adoption is plateauing — and the multiple compression will be swift. The market demonstrated this dynamic in real time during Q4 2025 earnings season. Meta, which showed concrete evidence of AI boosting ad revenue, saw its shares surge. Microsoft, despite beating on earnings and revenue, saw its stock punished because Copilot monetization was progressing slower than expected. Investors aren’t rewarding AI spending anymore. They’re rewarding AI earning. That shift in sentiment is the single most important development in tech investing heading into the second half of 2026. The $100 billion Nvidia-OpenAI infrastructure deal exemplifies both sides of the argument. It represents the largest AI infrastructure commitment in history and signals extraordinary confidence in future demand. But it also represents a bet that requires AI compute demand to grow at rates that have no precedent in enterprise technology adoption. There’s a subtler dynamic at work that neither bulls nor bears discuss enough. The earnings growth gap between the Magnificent Seven and the rest of the S&P 500 is expected to narrow significantly in 2026. The Magnificent Seven’s projected growth of 22.8% is still impressive, but it’s decelerating from much higher rates in 2024 and 2025. Meanwhile, the other 493 S&P 500 companies are projected to grow earnings at 12.1% — not spectacular, but the gap is closing. If that convergence continues, the rotation out of mega-cap tech and into the broader market could accelerate regardless of whether AI spending pays off. The broader enterprise technology landscape is being reshaped by embedded AI, and the beneficiaries increasingly include companies outside the Magnificent Seven that are using AI to improve margins without building billion-dollar data centers. This is where the valuation debate intersects with the governance challenges plaguing enterprise AI projects. If 40% of enterprise AI agent projects get canceled as Gartner predicts, that’s not just a problem for the companies deploying AI — it’s a problem for the companies selling the infrastructure. Every canceled AI project is a server rack that doesn’t get ordered, a cloud instance that doesn’t get provisioned, a line of revenue that doesn’t appear on the hyperscalers’ income statements. The honest answer to “are mega-cap AI stocks overvalued?” is that they’re priced for perfection in a world where perfection is unlikely. That doesn’t make them a sell, and it doesn’t make them a bubble. It makes them a high-conviction bet that requires continuous validation — and investors should treat them accordingly. Three principles should guide portfolio decisions in this environment. First, position sizing matters more than stock selection. The concentration risk in any portfolio that mirrors the S&P 500 is extraordinary — 30% exposure to five companies is not diversification, regardless of how strong those companies are individually. Second, watch the capex-to-revenue conversion rate quarterly. The moment AI capital expenditure growth materially outpaces AI revenue growth for two consecutive quarters, the multiple compression will begin in earnest. That hasn’t happened yet, but the gap is widening. Third, the best risk-adjusted returns in 2026 technology may come not from the companies building AI infrastructure but from the companies using it. A mid-cap enterprise software company that deploys AI to improve margins by five percentage points is a different — and arguably less risky — way to play the AI thesis than buying the hyperscalers at 28 times earnings and hoping the $680 billion bet pays off. The AI opportunity is real. The technology is transformative. The companies are extraordinary. But extraordinary companies at extraordinary prices aren’t automatically extraordinary investments. The market is right to be debating this, and the investors who will do best are the ones who resist the temptation to pick a side and instead focus on the math.

It requires macOS 15.6 or later, 8GB of memory (16GB recommended), and 7.2GB of available disk space. The iPad version requires iPadOS 18.6 or later and an iPad with Apple M1 chip or later, the base iPad (A16), or the iPad mini (A17 Pro). DaVinci Resolve is far more flexible. It runs on Linux (officially only Rocky Linux, but versions for Arch Linux, Manjaro, and Ubuntu enjoy substantial community support), macOS (macOS 14 Sonoma or later), and Windows (10 and later). Winner: DaVinci Resolve There’s no sugarcoating it: DaVinci Resolve’s interface requires some adjustment. That said, longtime video editors might be more comfortable with its more standard timeline tracks versus Apple's trackless Magnetic Timeline. DaVinci organizes major function groups across seven pages (in order): Media (gathering and organizing clips), Cut (quick assembly and edits), Edit (advanced editing and effects), Fusion (motion graphics), Color (color correction and grading), Fairlight (audio editing), and Deliver (exporting). The timeline in the Cut section works differently from that in the Edit section, and other pages’ node layouts demand a significant learning curve. Apple's Magnetic Timeline is easier on the eyes. Instead of tracks, Final Cut Pro uses lanes, with a primary storyline to which everything else attaches. This approach makes it easier to keep everything in sync than in DaVinci Resolve, but the latter does support linked clips. Auditions let you designate optional clips or takes for a spot in your movie, and you can group clips into compound clips—roughly the equivalent of DaVinci Resolve’s Nested Timelines. Roles include descriptive labels such as Dialog, Effects, Music, Titles, and Video. Winner: Final Cut Pro Both programs have strong media management capabilities. Resolve's tools are more traditional, while Final Cut Pro’s are simpler and more automatic. DaVinci Resolve starts you off in the Project Manager, before letting you organize media in databases called Media Pools that contain bins, power bins, and smart bins. Power bins are accessible across all projects, and smart bins are automatically created by the program based on metadata, keywords, and clip properties. Final Cut Pro organizes your media through events, keyword tagging, libraries, and roles. The library is the overarching container that includes your projects, events, and clips, and it keeps track of all your edits and options. You can also manage storage targets and perform batch clip renaming. The program’s new speech and object search are a boon if you want to be able to search for clips or sections of clips without applying metadata. Both DaVinci Resolve and Final Cut Pro work with pretty much every media format that you will need, along with those available via codecs or plug-ins for your OS. The list of supported files includes Apple ProRes, H.264, HEVC, and MXF, as well as raw formats from pro video camera manufacturers like ARRI, Blackmagic, Canon, Panasonic, RED, and Sony (some via plug-ins). Its node-based interface lets you create precise, complex effects and apply them to individual clips, clip groups, or the entire timeline. It's also possible to apply an effect to the original image or an effect node-modified version. With other software, effects simply stack, and you can’t control how they interact. Just keep in mind that nodes take a while to understand and aren't for simple hobbyists. DaVinci Resolve also supports the industry-standard Academy Color Encoding System (ACES), which Final Cut doesn't. DaVinci’s recent Chroma Color Warp feature lets you precisely adjust the hue and saturation of any color in your image by dragging points on a chromaticity diagram. But even nonprofessionals can benefit from DaVinci’s effective Auto Color tool. Color Wheels show a puck in the middle that lets you move an image toward green, blue, or red, with the result appearing off to the side. You can also adjust brightness and saturation with the wheels, separately control everything (with the Master wheel), or adjust just shadows, midtones, and highlights. If wheels are not to your taste, the Color Board option shows a simple linear view of your color settings. Title: AI could reshape 410,000 Silicon Valley jobs
URL: https://www.mercurynews.com/2026/03/01/more-than-400000-jobs-silicon-valley-exposed-ai-silicon-valley-index/
Time Published: 2026-03-01T12:00:12Z
Full Content:
This story is the second in a three-part series examining findings from the latest Silicon Valley Index. Read the first installment here. Robin McCarthy watches the images appear on her screen. Inside her San Jose architecture studio, she types a short prompt into an artificial intelligence program. Within seconds, it produces polished design concepts and photo-realistic renderings. “It’s exciting and scary at the same time, because you’re trying to figure out, ‘Is this going to affect my job?'” McCarthy said. “I like to think it won’t take away my role, but maybe one day it will.” That mix of awe and anxiety is spreading across Silicon Valley. Nearly 410,000 jobs in the region include tasks artificial intelligence can perform, according to the latest Silicon Valley Index, the annual report produced by think tank Joint Venture Silicon Valley. While many of those roles are expected to evolve rather than disappear, others could shrink as companies deploy systems that write code, draft legal documents, design marketing campaigns and analyze data in minutes. Unlike past waves of automation that displaced factory workers first, this technological shift is poised to hit the professional core of Silicon Valley’s economy. “What’s different is the exposure — it’s people at the high end of the economy,” Joint Venture CEO Russ Hancock said. The exposure is not evenly distributed. Households earning more than five times the federal poverty level — about $150,000 for a family of four — account for 19% of AI-aligned jobs, compared with just 5% among households at or near the poverty threshold, about $31,000 for a family of four. Because the Index looked at language and image tools typically used in office and professional jobs, the risk is concentrated in higher-paying roles rather than spread evenly across all workers. The Index groups AI’s employment impact into three categories: augmenting human work, restructuring how work gets done and replacing certain tasks or positions outright. The effects are expected to vary widely by occupation and even within the same job. The report identified dozens of fields with significant AI overlap, including architects, software developers, school psychologists, marketers and lawyers. At Project 100, a marketing firm with locations in San Jose, Oakland, Hercules and Las Vegas, AI now handles multiple functions once done manually, said founder and marketing director My Nguyen. The technology has accelerated communications with clients and reduced the time needed to build a website mockup from weeks to a few hours. Project 100 is also testing an AI chatbot version of Nguyen to conduct initial client consultations, he said. “Our competitive edge is we wanted to make decisions using patterns that a lot of people don’t see,” Nguyen said. “With AI, it’s a lot easier.” Nguyen sees the potential for AI to replace some marketers and graphic designers, but for now, he is using it to augment his staff’s work. “I had my team go back to school and learn these tools,” he said. Still, he expects “some consolidation” in the industry. At Costanzo Law Firm in San Jose, AI has saved “a tremendous amount of time” drafting documents, founding partner Lori Costanzo said. “Boom, it just spits it right out within five or 10 minutes, and that’s something that would take several hours for an attorney to do,” she said, adding that AI outputs still need to be reviewed by people. While Costanzo does not expect AI to eliminate attorney positions, “I could see it replace a paralegal,” she said. Not all work is easily automated. San Jose criminal defense lawyer Stephanie Rickard said much of her job depends on human interaction and judgment. “It’s face-to-face interaction,” Rickard said. “It’s negotiation, it’s knowing what’s important to a particular prosecutor or a particular judge in a particular fact set.” Silicon Valley’s concentration of software developers makes the region especially exposed. Roughly 140,000 residents work as software developers — more than a third of California’s total and the region’s largest occupational group, according to the Index. As AI tools have grown increasingly capable of writing code, worries have circulated that recent Bay Area tech layoffs may reflect workers being replaced by bots. “We have no evidence that they are AI layoffs,” Hancock said, noting many companies are still correcting for pandemic-era hiring sprees. However, Steve Blank, an adjunct professor of management science and engineering at Stanford University, said it appears low-level coders are being replaced by AI, though no reliable count exists. “This is an industrial revolution with all the social consequences wrapped in,” Blank said. “I think what we’re going to find out is it’s going to create new jobs, but possibly not at the same scale.” The 2022 release of OpenAI’s ChatGPT — generative AI capable of producing text and later audio and video — ignited a wave of investment and competition among Bay Area firms, including Google in Mountain View, Meta in Menlo Park and Anthropic in San Francisco, as well as global rivals. More recently, so-called “agentic” AI systems have emerged, allowing companies and individuals to create software agents that carry out multistep tasks with minimal human direction, from writing and debugging code to managing workflows. “You’re not typing computer code,” former Tesla AI chief and OpenAI founding member Andrej Karpathy wrote Wednesday on social media. “That era is over. You’re spinning up AI agents.” Those software agents heighten the potential for automation, said Shomit Ghose, a lecturer at UC Berkeley’s engineering school and a partner at Menlo Park venture capital firm Clearvision Ventures. An AI agent can learn the processes behind many white-collar roles and then “automate all these job functions,” Ghose said. “It never gets sick, never gets a day off, never gets tickets to the Niners game.” The anxiety is already shaping decisions beyond the workplace. Applications to San Jose State University’s software engineering master’s program fell 40% for the coming school year, and undergraduate applications dropped 16%, said Sheryl Ehrman, dean of the school’s College of Engineering. She said the declines appear tied in part to fears that AI will narrow job opportunities. “Our students are going to be prepared,” Ehrman said, though she expects the market to be “smaller than it was.” Oakland payments company Block announced Thursday it would cut 4,000 jobs from its workforce of 10,000. CEO Jack Dorsey said on social media that despite improving profitability and rising customer numbers, “intelligence tools” were helping deliver “a new way of working.” The company did not respond to detailed questions about how artificial intelligence factored into specific job cuts. Investors pushed Block’s stock price up 23% following the announcement. Dorsey later said the company had overhired during the pandemic. Nationally, job-search firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas reported in December that companies publicly cited AI as a factor in nearly 55,000 layoffs last year. Some analysts caution that economic realities may slow widespread displacement. A Feb. 24 report by financial services firm Citadel Securities said large-scale replacement of white-collar workers would require vast computing capacity. If the costs of chips, data centers and energy exceed the price of human labor for certain tasks, a “natural economic boundary” could limit how far automation goes. AI firms are racing to expand that capacity, spending hundreds of billions of dollars annually to build infrastructure in pursuit of what many believe will be a transformative market. As AI reshapes work at accelerating speed, those in AI-exposed occupations are wrestling with what may be lost. Architect McCarthy worries widespread adoption could diminish ingenuity and artistic vision. Lawyer Costanzo fears young attorneys may never fully develop foundational skills if drafting becomes automated. Marketing executive Nguyen foresees a flood of AI-generated materials that erode quality if used without human judgment. The uncertainty now extends beyond individual professions and into the region’s broader economic future. “How do we balance profitability with the need for gainful employment?” said Ghose, the UC Berkeley lecturer. “Employment is what pays taxes, employment is what drives purchases at the restaurant, and flowers for Mom on Mother’s Day, and new shoes for the kids.”
