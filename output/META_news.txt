Title: Google, Meta and the AI ‘hyperscalers’ are on a $1 trillion borrowing binge after years of printing cash. Here’s why Big Tech’s pivot to debt matters
URL: https://fortune.com/2026/03/07/big-tech-trillion-dollar-borrowing-ai-century-bonds/
Time Published: 2026-03-07T10:04:00Z
Full Content:
Almost every major capital spending boom during the past 200 years has ended in bankruptcies, consolidations, and tears—but also wins for the victors. The late 1990s buildout of fiber-optic networks, in which companies spent billions to pull dark fiber across continents and under oceans, saw borrowers like WorldCom, Global Crossing, and others go under. The shale revolution that prompted U.S. oil and gas companies to issue $350 billion in debt to fund drilling led to hundreds of bankruptcies after oil prices swooned in 2014 and 2015. Going back even further to the early 1900s, the widespread adoption of electric power led to a buildout that saw roughly half of the 3,000 small utilities and power companies that existed either disappear or get sold during a brutal decade of consolidation. In each case, there were also long-term victors who inherited infrastructure and reaped the benefits of these expansions in the form of lower-cost bandwidth, cheaper consumer prices, and large manufacturers that consolidated the power grid. Now, it’s AI’s turn. The artificial intelligence buildout is being driven primarily by five hyperscalers—Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Oracle—and has effectively become a capital-expenditure sprint with an eventual price tag expected to be in the trillions, most of it committed to constructing the massive data centers and cloud infrastructure AI requires. The fab five have thus far made total commitments of $969 billion, with more than two thirds, $662 billion, planned for data center-related leases yet to start, according to a Moody’s analysis published last month. Much of the buildout is being paid for with operating cash flows, but the sheer magnitude of the spending has prompted companies to shake up the calculus by bridging the gap between capex and free cash flow with bonds. In 2025, Alphabet, Amazon, Oracle, Meta and Microsoft issued about $121 billion in new debt via bonds, compared to $40 billion in 2020. And the pace is not expected to slow down anytime soon: Wall Street estimates show the AI-related bond supply could be in the range of $100 billion to $300 billion this year. Over the next three to five years, total data center investment could run $1.5 trillion to $3 trillion, according to some analyses. The trend is already changing the stakes for businesses that have traditionally had no need to borrow, introducing a new layer of stakeholders, obligations, and risks that are transforming how internet companies operate and how they are valued by investors. Bond investors, unlike equity investors, don’t seek out unlimited upside, they focus on being compensated fairly for taking on risks, including those related to overinvestment that leads to a glut in supply. “Any kind of large capital expenditure cycle that we have seen over history at some point leads to the risk of overinvestment,” said Mohit Mittal, chief investment officer of core strategies at global bond fund manager Pimco, which has about $2.3 trillion in assets under management. “There may be some form of over investment over the next two years that leads to a correction or a growth slowdown.” The debt-fueled AI buildout also changes the financial profile for some internet companies. “In an asset-light model, you tend to have higher equity multiples, and in an asset-rich model, you have multiples that are a little lower,” Mittal said. A flurry of deals at the end of 2025 saw Alphabet, Oracle, and Meta sell more than $70 billion in bonds over the course of weeks. That was in an overall investment-grade bond market that saw nearly half a trillion flow into taxable bond funds last year with foreign investors picking up about $304 billion in U.S. corporate bonds, according to asset manager Breckinridge Capital. Kevin SigRist, chief investment officer of the $143 billion North Carolina pension system and a significant long-duration corporate bond buyer, said the yields for the hyperscalers’ bonds are near 5%, which is attractive on its own before factoring in the strong balance sheets and corporate profitability. Yet, SigRist says the NC pension system remains generally underweight the sector relative to benchmark.“ The issue for us is the spreads are very, very tight,” he said. “And as you go longer, there’s not much of a yield pickup at all.” Still, the hyperscalers appear to be attractive to the two traditional buyers of ultra-long maturity bonds: corporate pension funds and life insurance companies. That pool of demand is ideally suited for hyperscalers with top credit ratings (four of the five hyperscalers carry credit ratings on the investment-grade elite spectrum) and bonds stretching 30 or 40 years into the future. In February, Alphabet even issued a rare 100-year bond, becoming the first tech company to do so in decades. More details about the buyers of these bonds will be revealed in the spring and later this year, when investors publish disclosures, but SigRist expects the wave of issuances to be well absorbed in 2026, much like it was in 2025. “The fact that investors are comfortable taking down 30-and 40-year debt, in some cases 100-year debt, certainly suggests that investors are very comfortable that this is a balanced risk-reward opportunity,” says Anders Persson, chief investment officer and global head of fixed income at $1.4 trillion manager Nuveen. In the case of Alphabet, for instance, long-term debt jumped from $10.9 billion at the end of 2024 to $46.5 billion at the end of 2025, but its total cash at the end of 2025 was $126.8 billion. Measuring total obligations to market cap of about $3.6 trillion, you get about 3.4%, meaning the obligations are just above 3% of the company’s market cap, even in a conservative scenario where total obligations include future, not-yet-commenced leases. Nuveen’s Persson, who was a tech analyst during the dot-com era on fixed income, has the benefit of hindsight as he assesses the current situation. Most of the issuers back then had no free cash flow and in some cases, no revenue. “It truly was a bubble that ended up bursting because this was, at the time, a brand new kind of opportunity, and the issuers that came to market were basically startup companies,” Persson said. It’s a world away from the likes of Alphabet, Microsoft, Amazon and Meta, which are sitting on balance sheets built like fortresses, and until the recently announced capex spike for 2026, were generating strong free cash flows. Even a significant misallocation of capital wouldn’t threaten the solvency of companies with the financial profiles of Alphabet or Microsoft. “It’s different this time, which is obviously a bit of a cliché, but for now at least, this is being approached quite prudently,” said Persson. Among the five hyperscalers, Oracle is the outlier with a Baa2 credit rating, which is just two rungs above so-called junk bond territory. Typically, the lower the rating, the greater the probability of default and the more yield bond issuers have to offer to attract buyers. Essentially, credit investors want to be paid more to own the risk of a company like Oracle versus Alphabet or Microsoft. Oracle has already leaned in on debt, relative to the other hyperescalers. The company has disclosed more than $248 billion of not-yet-commenced data-center lease commitments and it has borrowings of about $124 billion. Last year, Oracle issued $25.8 billion in notes with maturities dating to 2065 and last month pledged to raise $45 billion to $50 billion more this year, split between debt issuance and equity. On Thursday, Bloomberg reported that Oracle was planning to layoff thousands of employees as it grapples with a cash crunch to finance its data center build out. Oracle declined to comment. One problem with these mammoth capital cycles is that they create their own momentum with competitors following each other into larger and larger investments because the cost of being wrong models out smaller than the cost of being left behind if it all succeeds as planned. The looming risk is in the aggregate however, as history almost always shows that more gets built than the market can immediately absorb. Credit rating service Moody’s warned investors in February that on-balance sheet debt as well as economic debt related to not-yet-begun leases should be on investors’ radar as they think about risk. For instance, Alphabet and Meta, which carry Aa2 and Aa3 ratings, had to pay 10-15 basis point premiums over their existing debt to get their deals done, an analysis from Janus Henderson notes. “The pricing reflects both the scale of their ambitions and the market’s cautious stance on the amount of debt likely coming to the capital markets in 2026 and 2027,” the Janus Henderson authors noted. “In short, while debt is a more attractive financing source for hyperscalers, and credit investors remain willing to fund the AI revolution through numerous vehicles, relative compensation is required.” Indeed, for Persson, the Nuveem chief investment officer, the question isn’t whether there’s too much risk associated with buying the data center debt, it’s whether the bonds those companies are issuing to fund the buildout are priced to compensate investors for the full range of risks they’re taking on in a $969 billion commitment wave. Across the five hyperscalers, on-balance sheet debt is roughly $420 billion. The larger commitments live in the leases, including those yet to commence. Under the accounting rulebook known as GAAP, a lease commitment only winds up on the balance sheet as a liability if a company is “reasonably certain” to renew the lease, Moody’s noted. Much of that information lives in footnotes in financial filings, but bond fund managers are taking it all into account. On a gross basis without leases, hyperscaler leverage is marginally low, and they had more cash than debt as of 2025 year-end. Add in the leases and the figure is still low, but it’s less low than it was and it’s the first blush of something that could bloom into a concern. “We are incorporating these obligations and making those adjustments, particularly given the size of the leases and their growth potential,” Persson said. “Because ultimately, in our mind, these are commitments that they have to honor. We have to effectively view them as debt when we’re reviewing the credit quality of these companies.” And the fear factor is real in assessing the risk scenario and the debt the companies are taking on in issuing bonds. There’s economic debt versus balance-sheet debt to contend with, the shift from an asset-light model to an asset-heavy model, and the risk that this surge in spending won’t translate into revenues—or they won’t translate into revenues fast enough. The stock market appears to be moving on a hair-trigger any time there’s a spark of AI-related news, which means every quarter is a bit of a guessing game in terms of how stocks will respond. Ultimately, the companies leading the buildout have balance sheets that are likely strong enough to survive it if they get it wrong, unlike the shale bust, the fiber glut, and the vanishing of thousands of utilities. But we won’t know until after it’s already happened. “You only find out after the fact,” said Pimco’s Mittal. If you start to see it ahead of time, then others see it too, and investment starts to slow down on its own. “Every company will be quite different,” Mittal said. “There will be winners and losers in this environment.

Title: Booking CFO Touts 9/9/15 2026 Outlook, Expands Agentic AI Push at Morgan Stanley TMT
URL: https://www.marketbeat.com/instant-alerts/booking-cfo-touts-9915-2026-outlook-expands-agentic-ai-push-at-morgan-stanley-tmt-2026-03-06/?utm_source=yahoofinance&amp;utm_medium=yahoofinance
Time Published: 2026-03-06T23:35:53Z
Full Content:
Booking NASDAQ: BKNG CFO Ewout Steenbergen outlined the company’s medium-term financial framework and discussed regional growth priorities, marketing channel shifts, and the implications of generative AI and “agentic” travel tools during a fireside chat at the Morgan Stanley 2026 TMT conference. Get Booking alerts:Sign UpMedium-term targets and recent performance Steenbergen said Booking’s medium-term growth model is an “eight/eight/fifteen framework,” referring to expected growth of roughly 8% in gross bookings and revenue, and 15% in earnings per share. He said 2025 results exceeded that framework on a constant-currency basis, with “10%/10%/18%.” Booking Holdings Split: The Catalyst Wall Street Didn’t See ComingFor 2026, Steenbergen said the company’s guidance is above the medium-term framework, at “9/9/15,” indicating faster expected top-line growth while still targeting about 15% EPS growth. He attributed the model’s repeatability to travel markets generally growing faster than GDP, along with company-specific growth opportunities, operating leverage, and share repurchases. Regional focus: Asia, the U.S., and Europe On Asia, Steenbergen emphasized Agoda as the company’s primary Asia-focused brand, describing it as deeply localized in markets such as Korea through local user experience elements and payments. He also said Booking.com is investing in Asia and cited India as an example, noting that Booking.com recently added a domestic payment option and expanded beyond an agency-only model in that market, which he said opens growth opportunities. Steenbergen described Asia as expected to be the fastest-growing region over the next decade and said the company is focused on maintaining its position there. What a “Normal” Economy Could Mean for These 3 Travel StocksIn the U.S., he said the overall market is still growing at low single digits, while Booking has been growing at a low double-digit rate over roughly the past half year. He cited three drivers: Performance marketing optimization: Leaning into opportunities with attractive ROI through changes in bidding algorithms. B2B distribution: Growth from partners such as credit card companies and airlines that offer travel add-ons. Direct channel momentum: An increase in customers booking directly via the app or website, which he said began shifting around mid-2025 and reflects years of investment in brand and loyalty. Asked about alternative accommodations in the U.S., Steenbergen called the category globally important and said Booking has about 8.6 million alternative accommodation listings, up about 8% year over year, with faster growth in the U.S. from a smaller base. He said the company is investing in product features such as host-traveler communications and in expanding supply. On take rates and host economics, he said U.S. fee transparency rules have changed the “optics,” and that overall fee and commission presentation has become more aligned across market participants, reducing what he described as a prior optical disadvantage for Booking’s all-in pricing approach. On Europe, the company’s largest and most mature region, Steenbergen said Booking has been growing faster than the travel industry there and delivered high single-digit growth each quarter last year. He attributed Europe’s strength to the Booking app being a “go-to” planning tool, a broadening set of options across hotels, alternative accommodations, and flights, and loyalty value through the Genius program. He noted Ryanair was added as flight supply last year. He declined to break out direct mix by region but said markets where the company is stronger tend to have better direct mix, and added that Agoda’s direct mix may be higher than outside observers assume. Marketing mix and the rise of social channels Steenbergen said the company has increased performance marketing spend on social platforms beyond Meta, after building measurement capabilities to ensure incrementality and acceptable ROI. He said social spend was “a couple of $100 million a quarter,” and that it grew 13% year over year in the fourth quarter. He contextualized that within the company’s overall marketing and incentives budget, saying total marketing spend last year was a little over $8 billion. He also referenced merchandising incentives—treated as contra revenue—of roughly $2 billion, describing the combined pool as about $10 billion that the company continually reallocates based on returns. He added that large language models could become “diversifiers” of paid channels over time, though he characterized them as relatively smaller at present. AI, “agentic” travel, and the question of disintermediation Steenbergen argued that investor concerns about disruption from horizontal AI agents underestimate the complexity of travel fulfillment, including customer service, payments, tax and regulatory issues, and data privacy. He also emphasized the importance of trust and support when disruptions occur, giving an example tied to conflict in the Middle East and Gulf region and the need to assist travelers with cancellations, refunds, and rebookings. He recounted asking an LLM platform for help canceling and refunding a flight and said the response indicated the system could not directly perform the transaction, characterizing itself as a “travel strategy advisor, not the one pushing the button.” He said traffic coming from LLMs to Booking is currently “very small” and “not growing,” describing it as stable month to month. He said many users appear to use LLMs for research and itinerary-building, but still book through trusted travel platforms, while acknowledging that behavior could change over time. To protect and extend its direct relationship with travelers, Steenbergen said Booking is developing agentic tools across its brands to deliver an end-to-end experience “pre-trip, in-trip, post-trip,” including proactive itinerary adjustments, personalization, and coordination across trip components. He cited examples already in market, including “Penny” on Priceline, along with ongoing work at Agoda and Booking.com, and noted OpenTable has an AI concierge. He said investors should expect progress this year and suggested the conversation would look different a year from now as more tools become visible on the platforms. Operational savings and reinvestment plans On cost efficiencies, Steenbergen said Booking realized concrete savings in 2025 primarily through customer service, citing spend slightly below 2024 despite roughly 10% higher volumes. He said agentic tools reduced average handling time and the “contact rate” requiring human agents, and that average cost per booking declined about 10% while customer satisfaction held up. He said there is more potential in customer service in 2026, while other GenAI savings areas were “a little bit too early” to detail. He also reviewed the company’s transformation program and reinvestment approach. Steenbergen said the company increased transformation savings targets to $500 million to $550 million and achieved that by year-end 2025. He said Booking introduced a self-funded reinvestment program of $170 million in 2025 and scaled it to $700 million in 2026. He said the $700 million in investments are expected to drive about $400 million of additional revenue this year, supporting the company’s higher top-line growth outlook, with an approximate net bottom-line impact of $300 million. He added that the company expects about $250 million of additional in-year savings from the transformation program, which he said helps self-fund the reinvestment. In a discussion of AI hype, Steenbergen cautioned that widely cited figures about GenAI writing 30% to 50% of code do not necessarily translate to equivalent productivity gains across developers, given other parts of the software development process. He said the company intends to use increased development capacity to build more in the future. Addressing concerns about Google, Steenbergen described Google as a major partner and said Booking relies on real-time connectivity with 4.4 million properties. He noted that close to 90% of room nights booked on Booking’s platforms come from independent hotels, alternative accommodations, and smaller chains, and said the value proposition includes fulfillment, payments across more than 100 payment forms and more than 50 currencies, and tools that help suppliers manage how they appear and promote. Many groups are investing heavily in Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, and similar tools for using generative AI for software development, especially for actual code creation. I have played with Claude Code and OpenCode (for use with local Ollama models), and it is genuinely impressive what they can accomplish. The argument for leaning heavily on genAI is: it is cost-effective. One pundit wrote: And the economics are deliberately provocative: roughly ten dollars an hour of compute versus a developerâs cost of around $150 an hour. Uber, and to some extent Lyft, built their businesses up by spending investor cash on subsidizing rides: Uber passengers were paying only 41% of the actual cost of their trips; Uber was using these massive subsidies to undercut the fares and provide more capacity than the competitors who had to cover 100% of their costs out of passenger fares. Title: Why the next semiconductor shortage could be worse than the last one
URL: https://techpinions.com/why-the-next-semiconductor-shortage-could-be-worse-than-the-last-one/
Time Published: 2026-03-06T15:00:00Z
Full Content:
The global semiconductor industry hit $791.7 billion in revenue in 2025 and is on track to cross $1 trillion in 2026 — the first time in history. NVIDIA alone surpassed $100 billion in chip sales last year, accounting for over 35% of the industry’s total growth. But behind the record-breaking revenue numbers is a structural supply crisis that’s already rippling through every corner of the technology market. High-bandwidth memory is fully sold out through 2026 across all three major producers. TSMC’s advanced packaging capacity is oversubscribed through at least mid-2026. DDR5 spot prices have quadrupled since September 2025. And unlike the pandemic-era chip shortage, this one isn’t caused by supply chain disruptions — it’s caused by AI devouring a disproportionate share of the world’s silicon manufacturing capacity. The semiconductor shortage of 2020 to 2023 was a logistics problem — factories shut down, shipping routes clogged, demand spiked as the world went remote. The shortage emerging in 2026 is fundamentally different. It’s a structural reallocation problem, driven by the fact that the most profitable use of limited wafer capacity has shifted decisively toward AI infrastructure. Every wafer allocated to a high-bandwidth memory stack for an NVIDIA GPU is a wafer denied to the DDR5 module in a consumer laptop. That zero-sum math is now playing out across the entire memory and advanced packaging supply chain. High-bandwidth memory has become the single most constrained component in the semiconductor supply chain. The HBM market reached an estimated $30 billion in 2025, representing 23% of total DRAM revenue, and Bank of America projects it will hit $54.6 billion in 2026 — a 58% year-over-year increase. But the demand curve is steeper than the supply curve, and it’s not close. SK Hynix, which controls roughly 62% of HBM shipments, has told investors its entire 2026 supply is sold out. Micron’s CEO confirmed the same — HBM capacity for both 2025 and 2026 is fully booked. Samsung is raising HBM contract prices by high-teens to low-twenties percentages for 2026. And OpenAI’s landmark $71 billion order for Korean HBM chips in late 2025 illustrates the scale at which hyperscalers are locking up supply regardless of cost. The core problem is physics. Manufacturing one gigabyte of HBM consumes roughly three times the wafer capacity of standard DDR5. AI workloads are projected to consume nearly 20% of global DRAM wafer capacity in 2026, yet HBM and AI-related chips represent less than 0.2% of total chip volume while generating roughly 50% of semiconductor revenue. That concentration — enormous revenue from tiny volume requiring outsized manufacturing resources — is what makes this shortage structurally different from anything the industry has faced before. Even if memory supply were unlimited, AI chip production would still be constrained by advanced packaging. TSMC’s CoWoS (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) technology is the critical process for assembling the most advanced AI processors, and the scale of investment flowing into AI infrastructure has overwhelmed available capacity. TSMC’s CoWoS capacity grew from roughly 13,000 wafers per month at the end of 2023 to 75,000 to 80,000 by late 2025 — a sixfold increase in two years. The company is targeting 120,000 to 130,000 wafers per month by the end of 2026, with additional outsourcing of 240,000 to 270,000 wafers annually to packaging partners Amkor and SPIL. But even this aggressive ramp isn’t enough. TSMC executives have been unusually direct in stating that CoWoS capacity remains sold out through 2026, and NVIDIA — which has secured an estimated 60% of total CoWoS allocation — confirmed the oversubscription extends through at least mid-2026. The allocation hierarchy tells the story. NVIDIA takes roughly 60% of CoWoS capacity for its Blackwell and upcoming Rubin architectures. Broadcom takes approximately 15% for custom AI ASICs it builds for Google and Meta. AMD secures about 11% for its Instinct MI350 and MI400 series. That leaves less than 15% for every other customer — including the growing number of companies designing custom AI silicon. For second-tier clients, getting CoWoS allocation has become as much a strategic procurement challenge as a technical one. The structural reallocation toward AI is now visibly affecting consumer electronics in ways that most coverage of the AI boom hasn’t adequately captured. DRAM prices have risen 171% year-over-year. DDR5 spot prices have quadrupled since September 2025. Adata described the situation in late 2025 as a simultaneous shortage of DRAM, NAND, and HDDs — something the company said hadn’t happened in 30 years. The downstream effects are concrete. Dell, Lenovo, and HP have signaled PC price increases of 15% to 20% in early 2026 as memory costs surge. Memory now accounts for roughly 18% of a new PC’s bill of materials — approximately double its 2024 share. IDC projects that the global PC market could contract by 5% to 9% as a result, with the smartphone market facing a 3% to 5% contraction under moderate scenarios. Lenovo’s CFO described the cost surge as “unprecedented” and disclosed that the company had built memory inventories to roughly 50% above normal levels in anticipation of further price increases. Morgan Stanley downgraded Dell from overweight to underweight in late 2025, citing the company’s heavy exposure to rising server memory costs. The warning was blunt: skyrocketing memory prices could significantly erode margins for server and PC OEMs that lack the pricing power to pass costs through to enterprise customers with existing contracts. In previous semiconductor cycles, shortages resolved themselves through a predictable mechanism: high prices attracted investment, new capacity came online, and supply eventually caught demand. That mechanism is breaking down for three reasons. First, building new semiconductor fabrication capacity takes three to five years from groundbreaking to production. The US push to boost domestic chip production through the CHIPS Act is real, but the new fabs won’t materially affect supply until 2028 at the earliest. The physics of semiconductor manufacturing don’t compress to match the urgency of AI deployment timelines. Second, the economics of HBM and advanced packaging create perverse incentives for memory manufacturers. Why expand DDR5 capacity at commodity margins when HBM sells at a five-times premium? Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron are all rational actors, and they’re all making the same calculation — invest in AI memory where margins are highest and let consumer memory pricing float upward. The AI-driven surge in data center demand has permanently altered the investment calculus for memory manufacturers. Third, the demand side shows no sign of moderating. Hyperscaler capital expenditure is approaching $600 billion in 2026, a 36% year-over-year increase. Technology companies including Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta have placed what amount to open-ended memory orders — they’ll take as much supply as available regardless of cost. When the buyers with the deepest pockets are price-insensitive, the market-clearing mechanism that normally resolves shortages simply doesn’t function. For investors, the semiconductor shortage creates a bifurcated landscape. Companies positioned on the supply side of AI infrastructure — TSMC, SK Hynix, NVIDIA — benefit directly from scarcity pricing. The enterprise technology landscape in 2026 is being reshaped by who can secure allocation and who can’t. For enterprises, the implications are more immediately painful. Server lead times are extending. Memory costs for enterprise infrastructure are rising at rates that Deloitte’s 2026 semiconductor outlook describes as creating genuine budget pressure for IT departments. Companies that delayed hardware refresh cycles are now facing both higher prices and longer wait times — the worst combination for capital planning. The most concerning signal is what’s happening at the margin. Memory module retailers in Japan and Europe are rationing stock. Taiwanese distributors are bundling DRAM with motherboards to control allocations. Team Group’s general manager expects availability to worsen through the first half of 2026 as distribution stockpiles are exhausted, warning that obtaining allocation could become difficult regardless of willingness to pay. The semiconductor industry is entering what some analysts call a “gigacycle” — a multi-year structural expansion driven by AI that could push annual semiconductor revenue from $792 billion in 2025 to more than $1 trillion by year-end 2026 and potentially $2 trillion by 2036. But record revenue doesn’t mean balanced supply. It means a market where AI infrastructure consumes an ever-larger share of finite manufacturing capacity, and everyone else — from PC makers to smartphone manufacturers to enterprise IT departments — competes for what’s left. The shortage isn’t a glitch in the system. It is the system, reshaped by AI’s insatiable demand for silicon.

The question worth answering is whether the math supports him. The case for undervaluation rests on a specific framework called the PEG ratio, which stands for Price-to-Earnings-to-Growth. It’s one of the most useful tools for evaluating whether a high-multiple growth stock is actually expensive or just looks that way. The formula divides the forward P/E ratio by the expected earnings growth rate. A PEG ratio below 1.0 is generally considered undervalued for a growth company. Above 2.0 typically signals that the market is pricing in perfection. Broadcom’s numbers are striking on this measure. The stock’s forward P/E sits at 31.35, which sounds elevated until you factor in the growth rate. With Q1 AI revenue growing 106% year-over-year and total revenue up 29.5% in the most recent quarter, the earnings growth rate is running well ahead of the multiple. The result: a PEG ratio of 0.87, comfortably below 1.0. By this measure, Broadcom is not just reasonably priced for a high-growth semiconductor company — it’s cheap relative to its own growth trajectory. The catch Cramer acknowledged himself: macro conditions can override fundamentals in the short term. “I don’t know what’s going to happen with the war because if oil goes up, this thing goes down.” That’s an honest qualifier. A PEG ratio doesn’t protect you from a risk-off rotation or a sudden spike in energy prices compressing growth multiples across the board. But it does tell you that the business itself is not the problem. Broadcom peaked near $414 in late 2025 and has since pulled back to around $330, a decline of 18.47% from the December 11 close. That kind of drop on a company posting record results isn’t a business story — it’s a sentiment story. The market priced in aggressive expectations, the Q2 guidance of approximately $22.0 billion came in below the most optimistic analyst projections, and some investors rotated out. The underlying business didn’t slow down. Free cash flow came in at $8.01 billion for the quarter, representing roughly 41% of revenue — an unusually high conversion rate that reflects how little incremental capital the business needs to grow. That cash generation is what funds the $10 billion share repurchase program authorized on March 4, 2026, and it’s also what makes the consensus analyst price target of $453.06 plausible — roughly 37% above current levels. Of the 50 analysts covering the stock, 48 rate it a buy or strong buy, with zero sell ratings. Cramer also pointed to analyst estimate revisions as a signal: “I saw one analyst, he raised his Broadcom 2027 estimates by 24%.” Forward estimate increases of that magnitude, driven by a single earnings print, are meaningful. They suggest the growth trajectory is being revised higher, not lower — which is the opposite of what a value trap looks like. An investor with a 3-to-5 year horizon who can tolerate semiconductor cyclicality is the profile where this analysis holds up cleanly. Broadcom’s AI revenue has grown from roughly $4.4 billion per quarter to a guided $10.7 billion in Q2 FY2026, and the company’s custom AI accelerator business is tied to hyperscale customers like Meta, Anthropic, and OpenAI. That’s not speculative demand — it’s contracted infrastructure buildout. The quarterly earnings growth rate of 188% year-over-year reflects that reality. The investor who should be cautious is someone with a shorter time horizon or low tolerance for concentration risk. Broadcom generates enormous revenue, but a meaningful share of it comes from a small number of large customers. If one of those hyperscalers shifts its AI chip strategy — toward in-house silicon, for example — the revenue impact would be immediate and visible. The trailing P/E of 66.57 means there’s no margin of safety in the traditional value sense. The entire thesis depends on growth continuing at an elevated rate. The PEG ratio is the right starting point, but it requires an honest estimate of forward earnings growth. Broadcom’s current growth rate is elevated because AI infrastructure spending is in an early, aggressive phase. If that spending plateaus or slows, the PEG ratio will look less favorable quickly. Before buying on the Cramer thesis, run two scenarios: one where AI revenue growth continues at current rates through fiscal 2027, and one where it decelerates to 30-40% annually. In the first scenario, the forward multiple compresses significantly as earnings catch up to price. In the second, the stock is fairly valued but not cheap. The $10 billion buyback is a concrete data point worth weighting. Management teams don’t authorize large repurchase programs when they believe the stock is overvalued. Combined with four consecutive quarters of EPS beats and a cash balance that grew 52% year-over-year to $14.17 billion, the financial profile supports the undervaluation argument — at least on a growth-adjusted basis. Cramer’s call is grounded in the right framework. The PEG ratio, cash generation, and analyst consensus all point in the same direction — the risk here is macro and customer concentration, not the underlying business. Title: Navigating the rise of finfluencers: a multidimensional quality framework for strategic partnerships
URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1057/s41264-025-00334-7
Time Published: 2026-03-04T16:15:37Z
Full Content:
In the film Wall Street: Money Never Sleeps (2010), the antihero Gordon Gekko reflects on the moral ambiguity of modern financial services, stating: “(.) the beauty of the deal [taking other people’s money]: no one is responsible”. This phrase is gaining new relevance as financial influencers (finfluencers) are increasingly filling the financial advice gap by operating on various social platforms within a legal gray zone and with limited accountability (Barrett 2022; Moss et al. 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023). Even though there is no common definition yet, we follow Mölders et al. (2025), who define finfluencers as “personal brands who build relationships with an engaged audience through a regular flow of consistent, authentic and distinctive content posted on at least one social media platform that relates to, but is not limited to, personal finance and investment tips.”Footnote 1 While finfluencers can be seen as a distinct subset of expert-oriented and utilitarian influencers, their approaches diverge significantly: some position themselves as expert voices, drawing on formal qualifications, licenses, or professional experience to establish authority, while others adopt a utilitarian role, providing simplified, accessible financial tips for lay audiences (Mölders et al. 2025).Footnote 2 This dual positioning highlights the tension between perceived expertise and actual credentials, as well as the differing levels of accountability tied to each role. Comparable distinctions have been noted in the wider influencer marketing literature (e.g., Campbell and Farrell 2020; Vrontis et al. 2021; Han and Balabanis 2024) and are similarly relevant for other regulated domains, including medicine and politics (e.g., Ding et al. 2023). The rise of finfluencers was fueled by a number of factors, including the continuous expansion of global internet and social media usage (We Are Social et al. 2025), the pandemic (Boyde 2023; Davies 2023; Fava 2023), the growing valuation of global influencer marketing exceeding $30 billion in 2025 (Statista 2025), and the rising demand for financial literacy and advice (Calcagno and Monticone 2015; Barrett 2022). While the investment volume driven by finfluencers is difficult to pinpoint, finfluencers are currently outperforming their peers in other influencer niches, with double the follower growth on platforms like TikTok and Instagram (Emplifi 2024). Indeed, they seem to have a significant impact on the financial decision-making of younger generations as recent surveys show: e.g., in the United Kingdom (UK), over 44% of Gen Z state using information from social media for investment decisions; in the United States (US), 37% of Gen Z say finfluencers play a major role in their investment decisions (FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023); and in Germany, more than half of Gen Z and Millennials view social media as an alternative to traditional financial advice (German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority [BaFin] 2024). However, finfluencers tend to concentrate on high-risk asset classes such as cryptocurrencies, where social media hype and fear of missing out (FOMO) often drive investment decisions (Yamamoto et al. 2020; FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023; Merkley et al. 2024). A similar phenomenon has been observed in NFTs (non-fungible tokens), where pricing and trading volumes are closely tied to finfluencer activity rather than fundamentals (e.g., Kapoor et al. 2022; Horky et al. 2023). Thus, finfluencers can also be seen as facilitators of speculative retail investing (Mölders et al. 2025) and the crypto ecosystem. This is consistent with headlines such as “Eight Twitter finance gurus charged with criminal pump-and-dump scheme” (Robertsen 2022), which highlight the financial risks associated with “finfluencing charlatans” (adapted from Berk and Binsbergen 2022). In this prominent case, also known as Atlas Trading, eight Twitter-based finfluencers were indicted by US authorities for stock manipulation and defrauding retail investors of over $100 million (Securities and Exchange Commission [SEC] 2022b; US Department of Justice 2022). Beyond this, there have been numerous cases of fraudulent promotion of financial services (also by celebrities) and “get rich quick” investment schemes via social media (e.g., Lukpat 2022; Michaels 2022; Davies 2023). In response to such significant financial losses and consumer harm, regulators and authorities around the globe are turning their eyes on finfluencers (e.g., Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets [AFM] 2021a); Australian Securities and Investments Commission 2022; BaFin 2022; SEC 2022a, 2022b; European Securities and Markets Authority 2023; Financial Conduct Authority 2023a, 2023b, 2023c; Securities and Exchange Board of India 2023). For instance, the German regulatory body has issued the following warning: “On social media, you will find real experts – but many self-proclaimed experts as well. Both types are also to be found among the financial influencers (known as ‘finfluencers’)” (BaFin 2022). It is therefore not surprising that current research predominately discusses consumer harm and protection and corresponding legal perspectives (e.g., Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Hall 2023; Khurana 2023; Ramaswamy 2023; Stefanou 2023; Guan 2023, 2024). Apart from the consumer protection perspective, finfluencers could also be integrated as strategic business partners (Enke and Borchers 2019; Borchers and Enke 2021). This is especially relevant for financial services marketing (e.g., Mensah and Amenuvor 2022; Ho and Amin 2023; Nguyen et al. 2024). However, businesses seeking strategic partnerships with finfluencers are at risk of damaging their own reputation by potentially collaborating with finfluencing charlatans (e.g., Campbell and Farrell 2020; McMullan 2023). In consequence, we perceive a gap in the current academic discourse regarding the quality of finfluencers, which has been addressed only one-dimensionally based on their investing performance (Kakhbod et al. 2023; Espeute and Preece 2024). In response, we address this gap and follow calls for further research on new technologies, especially social media and finfluencers (Bhaskar et al. 2023; Hudders and Lou 2023; Bapat and Vashisht 2024; Yeo et al. 2024) and provide a complementary solution to economically costly over-regulation (Berk and Binsbergen 2022). Thus, our study aims to derive a multidimensional finfluencer quality (FinQ) framework to guide finfluencers’ stakeholders to select high-quality strategic business partners. Hence, we formulate our guiding research question (RQ) as follows: RQ What are quality indicators of trustworthy finfluencers and how can these indicators be systematically integrated into a multidimensional quality framework for facilitating strategic partnerships? We applied a mixed-method approach to answer the RQ. In the first step, we conceptualized a preliminary FinQ framework based on existing theory. In a second step, we used 12 expert interviews to refine and complement the preliminary FinQ framework, which resulted in the identification of 40 quality indicators (N = 40). These quality indicators can be categorized into five quality dimensions (QDs): (QD1) Compliance, (QD2) Content and Communication, (QD3) Credibility, (QD4) Profile Structure and (QD5) Engagement. In a last step, the refined FinQ framework was validated in terms of practical applicability and operationalization by a subsequent multiple-case study selecting five German-speaking finfluencers on Instagram. While our study tackles quality uncertainty surrounding finfluencers, it also encourages businesses to form strategic partnerships with credible, high-quality finfluencers, particularly in financial services marketing. The FinQ framework supports the professionalization of such partnerships by enabling more informed selection decisions. While it cannot fully eliminate charlatans, it helps financial service providers identify trustworthy partners, avoid reputational risks, and raise standards over time. Beyond financial services, the framework can also inform regulatory efforts, consumer protection, and strategic collaborations in other areas, such as listed firms’ financial communication or governmental financial literacy campaigns. The business model of finfluencers involves referral and reward networks potentially leading to conflicts of interest (Campbell and Farrell 2020; Reingen and Kernan 1986; Verlegh et al. 2013). Such networks usually compensate finfluencers on a commission basis upon referral of a client for a specific financial service or product (e.g., by using affiliate links or promo codes) (AFM 2021b). Thus, we assume that finfluencers face moral hazard because of their conflicted business model (Arrow 1985; Alon et al. 2021). In this context, when the lemon principle, referring to the problem of quality uncertainty in markets with information asymmetry between buyers and sellers (Akerlof 1970), is applied to finfluencers (sellers), their stakeholders (buyers; here consumers or followers and businesses seeking partnerships) face quality uncertainty, wherein we assume finfluencers are better informed. As a solution, Leland (1979) found that minimum quality constraints or licensing requirements tackle quality uncertainty in high-skill professions (e.g., lawyers or doctors). This can be applied to finfluencers, where typically rather uninformed followers consume conflicted financial (unregulated) advice. However, regulation of finfluencers may also reduce competition in the financial advisory market, which is why regulating finfluencers is not always the best option for consumer welfare depending on the level of consumer harm (Berk and Binsbergen 2022). However, there is currently no literature that empirically assesses the regulation of finfluencers from an economic perspective. While moral hazard provides a useful lens to study commission-driven finfluencer activity, not all creators of financial content on social media operate primarily for personal profit. Many also share content to educate, foster communities, build their personal brand, or even for entertainment (Guan 2023; Mölders et al. 2025). This heterogeneity of finfluencers tempers the reach of the moral hazard argument but still reinforces the need for a quality framework that applies across diverse motivation profiles. FOMO has been identified as a key driver of financial decision-making of Gen Z (FINRA Foundation and CFA Institute 2023), which puts emphasis on how moral hazard stems not only from conflicted incentives on the supply side but also from emotional manipulation on the demand or consumer side. While FOMO is conceptualized as a utility-based reaction to perceived opportunity loss from an economic perspective (Kaddouhah 2024), it can distort rational financial decision-making. Recent empirical studies show that such appeals amplify impulsive, risky and short-term oriented financial decision-making (Friederich et al. 2024; Meyer et al. 2024; Symbiosis and Gandhi 2024; Mölders et al. 2025) and reinforce herding dynamics and overreliance on social heuristics leading to systematic underperformance (Kakhbod et al. 2023; Gerritsen and Regt 2025; Haase et al. 2025). These findings highlight the need to address not only information asymmetries on the supply side, but also the risk of behavioral biases on the consumer side when assessing FinQ. Ultimately, we aim to conceptualize a FinQ framework that helps (corporate) stakeholders navigate the largely unregulated space of financial advice delivered by finfluencers on social media platforms. In markets with information asymmetries, where the true quality of a provider is difficult to assess, such frameworks can reduce uncertainty and support informed decision-making (Stiglitz 2002). Building on this idea, a FinQ framework enabling benchmarking (Cook and Luo 2003; Aiken et al. 2004; Hörner and Lambert 2021) may not only incentivize finfluencers to meet defined quality standards but also foster transparency and comparability. As a result, it might also encourage healthy competition and introduce a form of peer pressure and accountability within the finfluencer market (Calvó-Armengol and Jackson 2010). Quality is described by the international quality management standard ISO 9000:2015 as the “degree to which a set of inherent characteristics of (…) [a service or product] fulfils requirements.” Following this customer-oriented definition, we need to identify quality requirements (or standards) to establish a quality framework for finfluencers. The role of such a “standard setter” can be taken on by different stakeholders including researchers (Soni Raleigh and Foot 2010). In a first step, to measure quality, we must define a set of quality indicators. Therefore, we employ the concept of key performance indicators (KPIs).Footnote 3 Therefore, measuring quality depends on meeting defined KPIs as focal units of the framework.Footnote 4 In a second step, such defined KPIs will be aggregated into quality dimensions (QDs), which will serve as the conceptual cornerstones of the preliminary FinQ framework. This approach is consistent with the grouping of performance metrics into strategic dimensions, as seen in frameworks such as the Balanced Scorecard according to Kaplan and Norton (1992). In addition to considering moral hazard and behavioral biases, FinQ assessment needs to account for an environment of regulatory ambiguity, where jurisdictional inconsistencies and limited enforcement capacity can enable non-compliant or even unlawful practices and emerging forms of financial misconduct (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Stefanou 2023; International Organization of Securities Commissions 2025). We acknowledge that compliance under conditions of regulatory ambiguity often operates in a gray zone shaped by evolving standards, limited enforcement, and a strong reliance on self-regulation. These broader structural challenges serve as contextual boundary conditions that reinforce the urgency of establishing transparent and stakeholder-oriented quality criteria. Against this backdrop, we introduce five QDs grounded in the literature that serve as guiding pillars for structuring the FinQ framework: First, compliance with regulation is an inherent prerequisite for quality and gatekeeping dimension for influencer selection, especially in the light of corporate strategic partnerships with finfluencers (Espeute and Preece 2024; Mölders et al. 2025). However, the extent of compliance is dependent on the context, with requirements varying according to the country and the applicable regulation, which often remain fragmented and evolving (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Pflücke 2023). In the development of the FinQ framework, we based our approach on the German and European regulatory framework (see Appendix A). However, such regulatory and legal framework as a basis for compliance requirements must be subject to review and adaptation in each country or market. Consequently, we established QD1 Compliance. Second, we observe that most qualitative KPIs tend to be holistically defined in the existing literature, which is possibly due to their rather limited accessibility and measurability. Thus, we were able to identify rather holistic KPIs such as “post quality” (content quality) and “professional communication” from existing literature (Gräve and Greff 2018; Gräve 2019). However, such KPIs need to be further broken down and refined to be measured accurately. Other connected more specific KPIs refer for example to “playfulness”, which refers to the level of entertainment (Kim and Baek 2022). In response to such holistic content-related and communicative KPIs we formulate QD2 Content and Communication. Third, we also find that “experience” (but also track record) and “expertise” is often associated with influencer quality (e.g., Gräve and Greff 2018; Kim and Baek 2022). This is complemented by KPIs such as “trustworthiness”, “authenticity” or even “attractiveness” of influencers (e.g., Chekima et al. 2020; Primasiwi et al. 2021; Wiedmann and Mettenheim 2021). We refer to attractiveness on a personal level, which has been shown to enhance perceived trustworthiness and expertise through halo effects (Ohanian 1990; Reingen and Kernan 1993). In this context, credibility emerges as a central concept in the literature (e.g., Belanche et al. 2021; Bi and Zhang 2023; Dhun and Dangi 2023). To address the perceived importance of the credibility of finfluencers, we accordingly specify the principal dimension QD3 Credibility. Lastly, influencer marketing practitioners mostly rely on well-established and easily accessible quantitative KPIs such as community size, engagement rate, target group fit, posting frequency, sentiment or impressions (Gräve and Greff 2018; Gräve 2019; Primasiwi et al. 2021). This is also consistent with major influencer audit or due diligence platforms such as HypeAuditor and Upfluence (Alain 2022; Komok 2024). Thus, we derive QD4 Profile Structure and QD5 Engagement. While QD4 assesses how effectively a finfluencer manages their content and cross-platform presence, QD5 focuses on quantitative engagement metrics such as community size and interactions. However, studies by Goldenberg et al. (2024) and Hinz et al. (2011) emphasize that reach alone is insufficient, as true influence depends on content–audience fit, credibility, and network dynamics. This is supported by Lanz et al. (2019) who show that mid-tier influencers can outperform macro-influencers in engagement and trust.Footnote 5 Existing influencer quality frameworks in marketing provide useful foundations for evaluating influencer effectiveness. Leung et al. (2022) highlight influencer selection as a key step, focusing on traits like network size, engagement, authenticity, and brand fit (Gräve and Greff 2018; Lou and Yuan 2019; see also Audrezet et al. 2020). While the FinQ framework reflects general influencer criteria via QD4–5, existing frameworks often overlook finance-specific factors, especially compliance, regulatory risk, and the pitfalls of popularity-driven metrics. FinQ addresses this gap by embedding domain-specific requirements, aligning quality assessment with the unique expectations of financial services. It is built on five core dimensions and over 15 KPIs, which are refined and validated in the following steps (see Table 1; Fig. 1). Conceptual framework Our research is intended to further refine and validate the conceptualized preliminary FinQ framework derived from existing literature. In response to the novelty of the finfluencer phenomenon, we follow an exploratory sequential mixed-method approach according to Creswell and Creswell (2023) for the framework development (see Fig. 2). Therefore, we refined the preliminary FinQ framework through in-depth qualitative analysis of expert interviews, followed by a validation for practical applicability and robustness through a multiple-case study following Dehghani et al. (2022). Research framework Therefore, in the first step, we discussed the preliminary FinQ framework with an expert panel to identify the necessary expertise, partially following the approach proposed by Negrin et al. (2022) and the guidance on qualitative interviewing of Edwards and Holland (2015). Accordingly, an interview guideline (see Appendix B) was derived, which was followed by subsequent pilot interviews (Majid et al. 2017). After a feedback loop, further participants were recruited through online research and direct referrals (Noy 2008). The recruitment process was continued until the point at which theoretical saturation was increasingly evident, as indicated by the level of relevance in Table 2 and the corresponding reduced number of new code categories (quality indicators or KPIs) added sequentially by new interview participants as shown in Table 4 (Hennink et al. 2017). We interviewed various participants, including but not limited to financial journalists, financial service providers, consumer advocates, and finfluencers, capturing multiple perspectives on FinQ to ensure stakeholder triangulation (see column “Stakeholder type” in Table 2; Campbell et al. 2020). The semi-structured expert interviews (59.5 min on average; 12 h in total) were conducted from November 2023 to January 2024. All interviews were held online and recorded using Zoom after verbal and tool-based consent. After recording, the interviews were transcribed using Sonix.ai and subsequently coded and analyzed using the qualitative data analysis software MAXQDA. While fundamentally following the grounded theory of Corbin and Strauss (2015), the focus of this research was to expand the identified quality indicators (KPIs) and refine the preliminary quality framework. In this context, we applied a hybrid deductive–inductive coding approach. The five QDs, derived from literature, were used as top-level thematic categories, while the specific KPIs emerged inductively from the data. Interview transcripts were thematically coded in MAXQDA, with potential quality indicators grouped under the corresponding QD through iterative refinement. Coding was led by one researcher and cross-checked by a second to ensure consistency and interpretive reliability. To further enhance robustness, the built-in generative AI of MAXQDA was used to support the refinement process (Morgan 2023; Zhang et al. 2023). In the subsequent validation, a multiple-case study of individual Instagram accounts (Vaus 2001; Yin 2018) tested the practical applicability of the conceptually derived FinQ framework.Footnote 6 A market benchmark from Zülch et al. (2024) was used to select and benchmark five German-speaking finfluencers with data as of January 2024 (refer to Table 3). The cases were selected based on their market position and popularity, entailing a filtering process (with a minimum of 1,000 followers) and track record (operating on Instagram for at least one year), following the guidance of Miles and Huberman (1994). To ensure variation, cases were selected by gender and topical focus to capture diverse finfluencer types and content strategies. Ten posts per candidate (including pinned and random) were independently assessed in a double-blind review, with discrepancies averaged after a pilot for pretesting and calibration. All expert interviews were conducted in German, and translated quotes are provided throughout the analysis to illustrate key findings (the original German quotations are available upon request). The refinement of the preliminary FinQ framework resulted in a comprehensive set of 40 KPIs (N = 40) within the five previously established QDs. Notably, the most frequently coded KPIs are: (QD1) advertising disclosure and licenses/permission, (QD2) accuracy/correctness, (QD3) expertise/qualification, independence/neutrality and reliability/social proof, (QD4) target group fit and (QD5) community size and reach. In the following, we will use chosen citations of the expert interviews to not only dive deeper into the qualitative data set, but also to support the refined FinQ framework (see Table 4). QD1 Compliance is seen as “(…) the highest priority, it is always the first starting point (…).” (Interviewee 2). Thus, adherence to “Compliance is (…) conditio sine qua non” (Interviewee 4) making it a fundamentally non-negotiable QD with a total of eight KPIs. Consequently, regarding strategic partnerships in the financial services industry, this means managers and practitioners should have “a basic fear, which is reputation at risk” (Interviewee 4). It should be ensured that there is no promotional collaboration with finfluencers who do “not [ensure] one hundred per cent compliance” (Interviewee 4). In response, Compliance therefore follows the “all-or-nothing principle” as gatekeeping dimension. Furthermore, “transparent disclosure of funding” (Interviewee 6) is relevant to avoid conflicts of interest and signal potential moral hazard to followers. In this context, transparency is a high priority regarding advertising disclosure: “if you get money from someone, make it transparent” (Interviewee 7). QD2 Content and Communication encompasses nine exclusively qualitative KPIs, which tends to be a more subjective QD. However, the experts consider “criteria such as variety, ease of understanding and perhaps entertainment value” (Interviewee 3) to be particularly relevant. On the one hand, this dimension primarily concentrates on “the quality of the content, i.e. how high is the quality of the content produced?” (Interviewee 8). On the other hand, the focus is also on “professionalism in communication, (…) [i.e.] how do I communicate with followers, (…) even also if I present myself elsewhere?” (Interviewee 5). Managers and practitioners should therefore pay particular attention to the professionalism of finfluencers when engaging in strategic partnerships. Finally, objectivity is highlighted as an extremely important quality indicator within this QD, even though the business model seems to be conflicted: “One of the key factors is neutrality, incorruptibility [of finfluencers]” (Interviewee 10). QD3 Credibility is seen as another critical QD: “But credibility is, I think, (…) the most important thing.” (Interviewee 10). Following the breakdown and refinement of the KPIs identified in the literature review, a total of nine KPIs were identified for this QD. The experts consider “the personal experience (..) [finfluencers] have [as] very, very important.” (Interviewee 2). In addition, “relevant prior education or self-taught through a track record” (Interviewee 12) are relevant for the experts to assess the credibility of finfluencers. In consequence, “the track record (…) and the (…) expertise [play] a very important role” (Interviewee 8). These KPIs should also be considered by managers and practitioners when screening finfluencers to engage in strategic partnerships. Notably, the experts also stress that “channels where you don’t have a face” (Interviewee 11) or channels “that are based (…) [in a third country] and hide behind masks or pictures” (Interviewee 5) are early warning signs not to engage in strategic partnerships because of reputational risks of potential charlatans. QD4–5 Profile Structure and Engagement mostly cover well-established quantitative KPIs (14 in total), where especially community size and engagement rate are seen as particularly relevant for strategic partnerships: But “We don’t just want to have (…) influencers who have a high reach, we also need good followers who have a high engagement rate” (Interviewee 12). However, some experts hinted not to overestimate QD4 and QD5: (1) as they mostly cover generally applicable influencer KPIs not specific to finfluencers, (2) popular finfluencers “take it all”, (3) dilution of relevance by bots and fake engagement, and (4) there is some overlapping and correlation with the other mostly qualitative QDs (as kind of “residual KPIs” as a consequence of QD1-3). In this context, for a large-scale analysis of high engagement rate outliers and their potential dilution through scams and fakes, especially on Instagram see, e.g., Narassiguin and Sargent (2019). Furthermore, for potential overlapping and correlation of quantitative “traditional” influencer KPIs, see Gräve (2019). We developed a FinQ scorecard based on the refined framework (see Appendix C), revealing diverse quality profiles and content strategies. While some finfluencers combined financial expertise with practical advice, others focused on lifestyle or niche audiences. One candidate was excluded for non-compliance, and another was lacking finance-relevant qualifications. Engagement rates ranged from 0.22% to 1.70%, placing our sample between the 3rd and 40th benchmark percentile, reflecting relatively low levels. This supports prior findings that larger audiences often correlate with weaker engagement, while micro and nano influencers tend to show stronger relative engagement (e.g., Kay et al. 2020; Conde and Casais 2023). Further, the validation demonstrates that the FinQ framework facilitates a differentiation between high-, medium-, and low-quality finfluencers, even though a larger-scale application would be required to establish a robust market benchmark. While individual dimensions (e.g., engagement only) alone provide limited insight, multidimensional integration of qualitative and quantitative indicators reveal distinct quality profiles. Therefore, high-quality finfluencers need to perform consistently well across all QDs, demonstrating strong compliance, expertise and credible communication, while lower-quality finfluencers tend to exhibit selective strengths (e.g. high engagement but weak credibility). This demonstrates the added value of the FinQ framework when it comes to distinguishing reliable strategic partners from those who are less credible, or even potential charlatans. In total, 28 of the original 40 KPIs from the refined FinQ framework were validated and operationalized for the scorecard used in the multiple-case study (see Table 4). To ensure empirical applicability, the selection emphasized practicability and measurability over exhaustive coverage. Specifically, (1) KPIs that were observable and assessable via publicly available social media content (e.g., posting frequency, platform consistency) were prioritized; and (2) KPIs that proved overly subjective, ambiguous, or infeasible for reliable cross-case assessment (e.g., sentiment, self-monitoring, or internal compliance structures) were excluded. Importantly, the removal of 12 KPIs does not imply diminished theoretical relevance; these indicators remain part of the broader conceptual framework but were excluded from scoring due to methodological constraints. A full list of excluded KPIs is provided in Appendix D. To accommodate the varied nature of the KPIs, the FinQ scorecard applies different scale types: binary checks for compliance thresholds, dichotomous ratings for “all-or-nothing” factors like formal qualification, Likert-type ratings for nuanced judgments by analysts, and benchmark-based scoring for quantitative metrics (e.g., engagement rates). This mix ensures rigor while allowing consistent cross-case comparison. We also acknowledge that engagement metrics (QD5) may be distorted by inorganic activity, such as bots or fake accounts, which are known to inflate financial discourse on platforms like Instagram (Narassiguin and Sargent 2019; Kraaijeveld and Smedt 2020). While our scorecard relied on publicly observable engagement data, future applications of the FinQ framework should incorporate bot audit tools and fraud detection to improve the robustness of engagement scoring. The validation demonstrates the FinQ framework’s practical utility and benchmarking potential. It allows companies to identify strategic partners offering a replicable and scalable foundation for finfluencer due diligence in financial services. To enhance practical applicability, we translated the validated dimensions into a concise due diligence checklist (Appendix E). This tool complements the scorecard by offering clear, actionable questions that support consistent, real-world assessments of finfluencers for managerial decision-making. We establish a multidimensional FinQ framework that extends current literature on finfluencers beyond calling for regulation (e.g., Pflücke 2022, 2023). While moral hazard and quality uncertainty persist (AFM 2021b; Arrow 1985; BaFin 2022), conflicted financial advice may still be preferable to none (Chalmers and Reuter 2020; Mölders et al. 2025). Accordingly, the FinQ framework offers a complementary approach to avoid costly over-regulation (Berk and Binsbergen 2022) by enabling stakeholders to distinguish trustworthy finfluencers from charlatans and promote informed consumer decision-making (Stiglitz 2002; Hörner and Lambert 2021). This approach can be reinforced through trust marks, peer pressure, and reward mechanisms that foster professionalization within the finfluencer domain (Cook and Luo 2003; Aiken et al. 2004; Calvó-Armengol and Jackson 2010). At the same time, social media platforms increasingly act as gatekeepers through algorithmic moderation and compliance enforcement (e.g., Schroeder 2025). Their incentive to tackle misleading financial content aligns with broader accountability and regulatory goals, and the FinQ framework could further inform automated “charlatan detection” or other platform-based screening approaches by outlining key dimensions for identifying low-quality or harmful financial content. In consequence, high-quality finfluencers can be powerful allies in promoting better investment behaviors by addressing under-confidence, financial education gaps and risk tolerance (Heo et al. 2021). Notably, they could also contribute to overcoming the barriers that consumers encounter when obtaining financial advice (Moss et al. 2022). This is enabled by audience segmentation based on cognitive styles, enhancing content effectiveness and long-term financial decision-making and literacy (Estelami and Estelami 2024). Ultimately, finfluencers might also impact consumer behavior, supporting the adoption of new technologies through both community-building and endorsements (Nguyen et al. 2024). Our FinQ framework promotes transparency and indicates quality, which is essential for the selection of trustworthy finfluencers for all kinds of strategic partnerships. Thus, our work supports stakeholders to identify relevant high-quality finfluencers with which to engage in partnerships, thereby reducing the risk of reputational damages from finfluencing charlatans (Singh et al. 2020; Hudders et al. 2021). Further, such stakeholders save internal resources and reduce their risk exposure through guiding their selection strategy of relevant finfluencers (Saima and Khan 2021; Ren et al. 2023; Venciute et al. 2023). To further increase managerial usability, we developed two complementary tools based on the validated framework: (1) a FinQ scorecard (see Appendix C) that enables systematic benchmarking (FinQ-Score), and (2) a due diligence checklist (see Appendix E) that translates the validated KPIs into practical binary (yes or no) questions. These tools empower managers to conduct efficient, transparent, and criteria-based finfluencer assessments. In consequence, we see three main stake-holding corporate beneficiaries: First, our work guides sales and marketing managers of financial services businesses to extend their social media engagement and to select relevant high-quality finfluencers as trusted voices and brand ambassadors to especially target the digitally savvy younger generations (Anand and Mishra 2022; Mensah and Amenuvor 2022; Ho and Amin 2023; Chikweche et al. 2024; Lom et al. 2024; Nguyen et al. 2024). Second, our work also supports publicly listed companies in selecting finfluencers for extending their financial communication strategies targeted at attracting younger retail investors to diversify their shareholder basis (Binder-Tietz et al. 2024; Mölders et al. 2025). Finally, our work offers insights to financial planners and advisers, enabling them to gain a deeper understanding of finfluencers as competitors that present a challenge to their current business model (Holger 2020; Tkachenko 2022; Fava 2023; Reiter et al. 2023). In addition to corporate beneficiaries, we find that our research could also support managers of regulatory bodies and policymakers to refine current regulatory practices and consumer protection even though we acknowledge that the FinQ framework can only complement rethinking the current regulation of finfluencers (Aggarwal et al. 2022; Miller 2022; Pflücke 2022, 2023; Guan 2023, 2024; Hall 2023; Stefanou 2023; Espeute and Preece 2024). Our exploratory research is inherently limited due to the dynamic regulatory and technological environment, but also particularly regarding the variable understanding of quality itself. Thus, future research should further differentiate how the FinQ framework handles various finfluencer types, as quality may mean different things for licensed advisors versus unqualified promoters of high-risk assets (potential charlatans). In addition, the FinQ scorecard itself offers the potential to profile such different types (e.g., highly professional, lifestyle-oriented, and entertainment-driven). Future studies could leverage these profiles to map the distribution of finfluencer types across markets and platforms, tracking how they evolve over time. Further, our research is limited by involving an in-depth but small qualitative sample. Also, while mixed-method approaches enhance triangulation of data, they can lead to biases (Campbell et al. 2020; Creswell and Creswell 2023). In consequence, we encourage future studies to employ larger-scale quantitative designs to validate, refine, and potentially generalize our findings, for instance, by testing the FinQ-Score against the actual performance of investment recommendations from a broader set of scored finfluencers. Furthermore, reliance on historical data may not accurately reflect status quo. Thus, further research could aim at keeping objectivity and up-to-dateness (e.g., using AI-powered solutions or follower involvement) and extend the FinQ framework into a longitudinal tool, enabling repeated assessments to capture shifts in reputation and content quality over time. In addition, platform differences present a challenge to applicability and validity of our multiple-case study highlighting the need for cross-platform research. Lastly, we were unable to include all KPIs in the refined FinQ framework due to feasibility constraints, lack of public assessability or observability, and the exclusion of overly subjective or redundant indicators. Moreover, our engagement scoring may not fully capture distortions from bots or fake accounts. Future applications of the FinQ framework should integrate bot-audit tools and fraud detection to enhance robustness. Additionally, our FinQ framework is based on German and European Union law, which might necessitate adjustments of the Compliance dimension for its application to other markets and countries. In response, we call for the development of “generally accepted quality standards for finfluencers” as an extension to our FinQ framework. Finally, future studies could evaluate the FinQ framework’s effectiveness by linking it to actual success metrics of strategic partnerships with finfluencers, such as lead generation, return on investment (ROI), or changes in brand trust. While promising, this step requires longitudinal or other designs that go beyond the scope of our study. Nonetheless, we highlight it as a critical future direction to assess the long-term impact and strategic value of high-quality finfluencer partnerships. Ultimately, other researchers may use our framework as groundwork and provide novel further insights into leveraging high-quality finfluencers as a distinctive opportunity for the financial services industry to cultivate trust, engage with diverse audiences, and develop tailored marketing strategies that resonate with younger consumers in the age of social media. Returning to the introductory quote of the antihero Gordon Gekko, finfluencers currently lack accountability, and their mostly commission-based business model involves moral hazard and behavioral biases which not only causes quality uncertainty, but also potential consumer harm and financial losses. In response, we acknowledge the need for reassessing their current regulation to protect consumers (e.g., through setting minimum quality constraints). However, we see a risk of overregulating finfluencers, which could be not only costly from an economic perspective, but also may take away the opportunity to integrate finfluencers as potential allies in corporate and governmental strategic partnerships. In response, our FinQ framework is a complementary approach to tackle the current lack of accountability. In this context, our findings could accelerate professionalization by enabling informed decision making to eventually drive finfluencing charlatans out of the market. Further, our research builds on improving trustworthiness as a basis for encouraging strategic partnerships with high-quality finfluencers. We also find that regulators and governmental bodies should uphold a direct dialogue with high-quality finfluencers to integrate them into policymaking and strategies on promoting financial education, inclusion and literacy. We find that finfluencers may prove to be more than a short-term phenomenon or a further source of noise within the capital markets. Rather, they may represent a long-term game changer for the digital financial community and the financial services industry.
