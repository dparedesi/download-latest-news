Sending You to Google News in 3 © TechAnimationStock / Shutterstock.com The VanEck Semiconductor ETF (NASDAQ:SMH) and the iShares Semiconductor ETF (NASDAQ:SOXX) appear to be substitutes on a fund screener. Both promise pure-play chip exposure, both charge roughly the same fee, and both ride the same AI cycle. The reason the choice still matters is that their indexes draw different lines around what counts as a chip stock and how much any one name can move the fund. In 2026, that distinction produced a return gap wide enough to reshape a portfolio. SMH tracks the MarketVector US Listed Semiconductor 10% Capped Screened Index, a 25-stock basket that limits any single position to 10% at each rebalance. The cap matters less than the roster, and as of June 9, 2026, the top weights are NVIDIA 15.55%, TSM 9.78%, Micron 7.28%, AMD 7.22%, and Intel 6.56%. SOXX tracks the NYSE Semiconductor Index, a portfolio of about 30 holdings that moved away from the old PHLX rules in 2021. It uses tighter weighting bands that tend to push more capital toward equipment makers such as Applied Materials, Lam Research, and KLA. SMH also owns those companies, but the index design gives SOXX a stronger tilt toward the capital equipment side of the value chain. The underlying bet is on broader-cycle participation rather than on the largest design winners, a distinction that shapes how investors think about semiconductor breadth and value chain exposure. It’s also important to note that SOXX’s NVIDIA weight is closer to 9% versus the more than 15% SMH holds. Through June 9, 2026, SOXX is up 86.78% year-to-date, while SMH has gained 64.11%. Over the trailing year, SOXX returned 160% versus SMH at 135%. The rally broadened in 2026, equipment names re-rated, and SOXX’s wider net captured more of it. Run the clock back, and the picture inverts. Over five years, SMH returned 391%, compared with SOXX at 307%. Over ten, SMH gained 2,148% while SOXX added 1,833%. That long-run lead reflects the years when NVIDIA and Taiwan Semiconductor carried the sector, and SMH’s heavier mega-cap tilt collected the upside. Fees are effectively tied at 0.35% and 0.34%. Both trade with deep options markets and tight spreads, so liquidity is not a tiebreaker for retail size. The structural wedge is SMH’s 9.15% Netherlands weighting through ASML and its 8.75% Taiwan weighting through TSMC; exposure to SOXX is diluted by design. SOXX fits the investor who wants exposure to the semiconductor cycle without hinging the whole fund on a few leaders, and who values equipment-maker exposure when the cycle broadens. SMH fits the investor who wants concentrated leverage to the largest design and foundry names and is comfortable giving up diversification to get it. The 2026 rally rewarded SOXX’s breadth, but the multi‑year record still belongs to SMH. The calculus flips when leadership narrows back to two or three mega caps, which is exactly the setup SMH is built to capture, the kind of environment that highlights concentration payoffs over broad cycle exposure. Title: Intel Is the Market's Most Mispriced AI Hedge
URL: https://www.marketbeat.com/originals/intel-is-the-markets-most-mispriced-ai-hedge/?utm_source=yahoofinance&amp;utm_medium=yahoofinance
Time Published: 2026-06-10T18:55:00Z
Full Content:
A quiet shift is underway in the global semiconductor landscape. While market-wide macro headwinds punish technology valuations, a foundational realignment of the AI supply chain is taking place. Alphabet Inc. NASDAQ: GOOGL has committed to a massive three-million-unit order for its Tensor Processing Units to be manufactured by Intel Corporation NASDAQ: INTC, a move that validates the chipmaker's ambitious foundry pivot and signals a strategic hedge against geopolitical risk. This is not a trial run; it is a structural change. For investors, the recent sector-wide sell-off has created a significant valuation disconnect, masking the long-term catalysts now locked into Intel Corporation's future revenue. Alphabet Inc.'s 2028 production order is the most tangible proof point yet of Intel Foundry's resurgence. This commitment represents an estimated 50% of Google's projected TPU output for that year, a clear indicator that this is a primary manufacturing allocation, not a secondary experiment. The decision appears driven by two critical factors: technology and geography. Technologically, Intel Corporation's advanced packaging is hitting key milestones. The Embedded Multi-die Interconnect Bridge (EMIB) technology, crucial for stitching complex chiplets into a single powerful processor, has achieved yield rates above 90%. This directly challenges the current market leader, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. NYSE: TSM, whose CoWoS packaging capacity is severely constrained by unprecedented global demand for AI chips. Hyperscalers require volume and reliability, and Intel Corporation's EMIB is now a highly viable, scalable alternative. The urgency for this diversification is underscored by Alphabet Inc.'s own operational pressures. The Gemini AI platform has seen explosive growth, now serving over 900 million monthly active users. This has stretched Google's internal computing resources to the limit, forcing it to secure auxiliary AI compute capacity from external partners like SpaceX at a reported run rate of $920 million per month. For Alphabet Inc., securing a high-volume, domestic foundry partner is no longer a strategic option but a core operational necessity to manage future growth and costs. Alphabet Inc.’s latest strategic move is creating a ripple effect across the industry, with other major players now seriously considering Intel Foundry services. This cascading validation is critical, as it signals a broader industry acceptance of Intel Corporation as a legitimate leading-edge manufacturer. NVIDIA Corporation NASDAQ: NVDA, the undisputed leader in AI accelerators, is actively evaluating Intel Corporation's 18A process node for a next-generation, multi-die GPU. Success here would be a monumental win for Intel Corporation, breaking into the highest-margin segment of the AI hardware market. The validation extends beyond traditional tech players. Tesla, Inc. NASDAQ: TSLA has committed to Intel Corporation's 14A process for the custom silicon powering its Terafab AI computing complex in Austin. This move by a leader known for demanding extreme performance and supply chain reliability adds another layer of credibility to Intel Corporation's manufacturing roadmap. The common thread is a strategic imperative among U.S. tech giants to de-risk their supply chains from potential disruptions in Taiwan by onshoring critical chip production. Intel Corporation is emerging as the primary beneficiary of this geopolitical tailwind. Despite these massive, long-term catalysts, Intel Corporation's stock price recently fell 8% in a broad, macro-driven rotation out of the technology sector. This price action is at odds with improving fundamentals. In its Q1 2026 earnings report, Intel Corporation posted revenue of $13.58 billion, handily beating consensus estimates by more than $1.1 billion. The outperformance was driven largely by strength in its Data Center and AI division, the very segment poised to benefit most from the foundry strategy. The market appears to be mispricing a temporary, inflation-driven sector rotation against a permanent, structural shift in Intel Corporation's business. While the forward P/E ratio of roughly 170X appears stretched, it fails to properly account for the immense multi-year order book being built. The Alphabet deal, combined with potential contracts from NVIDIA and Tesla, provides Intel with a level of forward revenue visibility it has not enjoyed in years. This situation presents a classic divergence for fundamental investors. The headline financial metrics, such as a trailing net margin of -5.90%, reflect the costly turnaround effort of the past, not the validated production pipeline of the future. The primary risk remains execution; Intel Corporation must deliver on its process technology promises on schedule and at scale. However, the commitment from exacting customers like Alphabet suggests confidence is building where it matters most: in the engineering clean rooms. For investors with a multi-year time horizon, the current weakness in Intel Corporation's share price may present a compelling opportunity. The market is reacting to short-term macroeconomic fears, potentially overlooking the fundamental re-architecting of the global semiconductor supply chain that is placing Intel Corporation at the center of America's strategic technological future. Investors might consider adding Intel Corporation to their watchlist, paying close attention to further foundry announcements and progress on its technology roadmap as indicators that the market is beginning to price in this structural shift.
