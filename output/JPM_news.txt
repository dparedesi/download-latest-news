Title: JPMorgan outlines the biggest forces that will shape markets in the 2nd half of 2026
URL: https://www.businessinsider.com/stock-market-outlook-risks-jpmorgan-inflation-labor-ai-wealth-effect-2026-7#article
Time Published: 2026-07-17T09:45:02Z
Full Content:
JPMorgan is eyeing a challenging second half of the year for markets as it scopes out the big forces that will be in the driver's seat in the months ahead. Strategists at the top US bank said they believe stock returns will be lackluster for the remainder of 2026, thanks to a handful of risks they see ahead for stocks. The S&P 500 will likely see "more modest" outperformance relative to international stocks through the rest of 2026, a team led by Zahin Ov, a senior associate at JPMorgan, wrote on Thursday. "We expect market upside from current levels but returns to the end of the year are unlikely to match 1H26 performance," the report said. It's been a wild first half of the year for markets overall, with stocks rocked by geopolitical tensions, concerns about AI disruption, and an ongoing rotation within the AI trade, all while managing to still post a solid gain for the first six months. The S&P 500 is up 9% for the year, having rebounded more than 18% from its April low as optimism has grown for a peace deal with Iran. Here are some of the biggest forces that the bank sees shaping the market through the rest of 2026: The bank pointed to a June report from the Bank of International Settlements, which highlighted vulnerabilities in the financial system related to how traditional buyers of government bonds were stepping back and being replaced by hedge funds, foreign investors, and private investors. Take a smarter break in your day - and see how far you get. "The BIS notes that risks have moved away from the traditional bank-sovereign nexus as hedge funds now intermediate government debt in several core sovereign bond markets, increasing the risk of market dysfunction," strategists wrote. Separately, markets could also see bigger swings than they have in the past, the bank added. "Structurally higher volatility is the new baseline, and shifts in market structure have contributed to heightened daily volatility with positions requiring faster adjustments," the note said. Hotter inflation and higher-for-longer interest rates also look like they could weigh on equities in the second-half. Inflation came in cooler-than-expected in June, but has been a key issue weighing on markets all year, given ongoing pressure from tariffs and higher energy prices from the Iran war. Consumer prices rose at a 3.5% annualized pace in June, a long ways from the Fed's 2% price target. The yield on the benchmark 10-year US Treasury, one reflection of inflation and long-term rate expectations in the economy, has climbed to around 4.56%, breaching the key 4.5% threshold. "At the same time, the gap between the equity market return and bond yields (Equity Risk Premium) has declined to a new post-financial crisis low, implying that there is limited room before a further rise in bond yields starts becoming a problem for the equity market," strategists added of the risks to stocks. Retail investors' heavy exposure to the stock market has amplified the "wealth effect," a phenomenon where people spend more money as stock gains boost their wealth on paper. The trend also raises the risk of a "downside feedback loop," the bank said, suggesting that economic activity could slow more suddenly than in the past if stocks were to take a hit. Equities now account for around a third of all household wealth in the US, a record high. "A major market correction could have much greater wealth effects than in the past. Compared to historic levels, both 2025 and YTD 2026 have seen retail trading activity at or near record highs," strategists said. The job market could be further disrupted by AI, especially if the focus shifts to job losses over productivity gains from the technology, JPM said. AI has been the leading cause for job cuts for four straight months, accounting for over 100,000 job cut announcements so far this year, according to a recent report from the consultancy Challenger, Gray & Christmas. Around 63% of Americans expect AI to continue to reduce the number of jobs, according to one poll conducted by YouGov. "Labor market's 'new normal' remains unsettled, with younger college graduates disproportionately exposed," the bank wrote.
