It shows in the numbers Before we dive into the data below, let‚Äôs put the most important question up front: Why have C++ and Rust been the fastest-growing major programming languages from 2022 to 2025? Primarily, it‚Äôs because throughout the history of computing ‚Äúsoftware taketh away faster than hardware giveth.‚Äù There is enduring demand for efficient languages because our demand for solving ever-larger computing problems consistently outstrips our ability to build greater computing capacity, with no end in sight. [6] Every few years, people wonder whether our hardware is just too fast to be useful, until the future‚Äôs next big software demand breaks across the industry in a huge wake-up moment of the kind that iOS delivered in 2007 and ChatGPT delivered in November 2022. What‚Äôs in shortest supply? Take a moment to answer that yourself before reading on‚Ä¶ ‚Äî ‚Äî ‚Äî If you answered exactly ‚Äúpower and chips,‚Äù you‚Äôre right ‚Äî and in the right order. Chips are only our #2 bottleneck. It‚Äôs well known that the hyperscalars are competing hard to get access to chips. That‚Äôs why NVIDIA is now the world‚Äôs most valuable company, and TSMC is such a behemoth that it‚Äôs our entire world‚Äôs greatest single point of failure. But many people don‚Äôt realize: Power is the #1 constraint in 2025. Did you notice that all the recent OpenAI deals were expressed in terms of gigawatts? Let‚Äôs consider what three C-level executives said on their most recent earnings calls. [1] Amy Hood, Microsoft CFO (MSFT earnings call, October 29, 2025): [Microsoft Azure‚Äôs constraint is] not actually being short GPUs and CPUs per se, we were short the space or the power, is the language we use, to put them in. Jensen Huang, NVIDIA CEO (NVDA earnings call, November 19, 2025): The most important thing is, in the end, you still only have 1 gigawatt of power. One gigawatt data centers, 1 gigawatt power. ‚Ä¶ That 1 gigawatt translates directly. Your performance per watt translates directly, absolutely directly, to your revenues. That‚Äôs why the future is enduringly bright for languages that are efficient in ‚Äúperformance per watt‚Äù and ‚Äúperformance per transistor.‚Äù The size of computing problems we want to solve has routinely outstripped our computing supply for the past 80 years; I know of no reason why that would change in the next 80 years. [2] The list of major portable languages that target those key durable metrics is very short: C, C++, and Rust. [3] And so it‚Äôs no surprise to see that in 2025 all three continued experiencing healthy growth, but especially C++ and Rust. Let‚Äôs take a look. Programming is a hot market, and programmers are in long-term high-growth demand. (AI is not changing this, and will not change it; see Appendix.) ‚ÄúGlobal developer population trends 2025‚Äù (SlashData, 2025) reports that in the past three years the global developer population grew about 50%, from just over 31 million to just over 47 million. JetBrains reports similar numbers of professional developers; their numbers are smaller because they exclude students and hobbyists.) And which two languages are growing the fastest (highest percentage growth from 2022 to 2025)? Rust, and C++. To put C++‚Äôs growth in context: C++ is a living language whose core job to be done is to make the most of hardware, and it is continually evolving to stay relevant to the changing hardware landscape. The new C++26 standard contains additional support for hardware parallelism on the latest CPUs and GPUs, notably adding more support for SIMD types for intra-CPU vector parallelism, and the std::execution Sender/Receiver model for general multi-CPU and GPU concurrency and parallelism. But wait ‚Äî how could this growth be happening? Isn‚Äôt C++ ‚Äútoo unsafe to use,‚Äù according to a spate of popular press releases and tweets by a small number of loud voices over the past few years? Let‚Äôs tackle that next‚Ä¶ C++‚Äôs rate of security vulnerabilities has been far overblown in the press primarily because some reports are counting only programming language vulnerabilities when those are a smaller minority every year, and because statistics conflate C and C++. Let‚Äôs consider those two things separately. First, the industry‚Äôs security problem is mostly not about programming language insecurity. Year after year, and again in 2025, in the MITRE ‚ÄúCWE Top 25 Most Dangerous Software Weaknesses‚Äù (mitre.org, 2025) only three of the top 10 ‚Äúmost dangerous software weaknesses‚Äù are related to language safety properties. Of those three, two (out-of-bounds write and out-of-bounds read) are directly and dramatically improved in C++26‚Äôs hardened C++ standard library which does bounds-checking for the most widely used bounded operations (see below). And that list is only about software weaknesses, when more and more exploits bypass software entirely. Why are vulnerabilities increasingly not about language issues, or even about software at all? Because we have been hardening our software; this is why the cost of zero-day exploits has kept rising, from thousands to millions of dollars. So attackers stop pursuing that as much, and switch to target the next slowest animal in the herd. For example, ‚ÄúCrowdStrike 2025 Global Threat Report‚Äù (CrowdStrike, 2025) reports that ‚Äú79% of [cybersecurity intrusion] detections were malware-free,‚Äù not involving programming language exploits. Second, for the subset that is about programming language insecurity, the problem child is C, not C++. A serious problem is that vulnerability statistics almost always conflate C and C++; it‚Äôs very hard to find good public sources that distinguish them. The only reputable public study I know of that distinguished between C and C++ is Mend.io‚Äôs as reported in ‚ÄúWhat are the most secure programming languages?‚Äù (Mend.io, 2019). Focus is about saying no. Programming is growing fast. C++ is growing very fast, with a healthy long-term future because it‚Äôs deeply aligned with the overarching 80-year trend that computing demand always outstrips supply. C++ is a living language that continually adapts to its environment to fulfill its core mission, tracking what developers need to make the most of hardware. And it shows in the numbers. Here‚Äôs to C++‚Äôs great 2025, and its rosy outlook in 2026! I hope you have an enjoyable rest of the holiday period, and see you again in 2026. Thanks to Saeed Amrollahi Boyouki, Mark Hoemmen and Bjarne Stroustrup for motivating me to write this post and/or providing feedback. Finally, let‚Äôs talk about the topic no article can avoid: AI. C++ is foundational to current AI. If you‚Äôre running AI, you‚Äôre running CUDA (or TensorFlow or similar) ‚Äî directly or indirectly ‚Äî and if you‚Äôre running CUDA (or TensorFlow or similar), you‚Äôre probably running C++. CUDA is primarily available as a C++ extension. There‚Äôs always room for DSLs at the leading edge, but for general-purpose AI most high-performance deployment and inference is implemented in C++, even if people are writing higher-level code in other languages (e.g., Python). But more broadly than just C++: What about AI generally? Will it take all our jobs? (Spoiler: No.) AI is a wonderful and transformational tool that greatly reduces rote work, including problems that have already been solved, where the LLM is trained on the known solutions. But AI cannot understand, and therefore can‚Äôt solve, new problems ‚Äî which is most of the current and long-term growth in our industry. What does that imply? Two main things, in my opinion‚Ä¶ First, I think that people who think AI isn‚Äôt a major game-changer are fooling themselves. To me, AI is on par with the wheel (back in the mists of time), the calculator (back in the 1970s), and the Internet (back in the 1990s). [5] Each of those has been a game-changing tool to accelerate (not replace) human work, and each led to more (not less) human production and productivity. (Spoiler: ‚ÄúObjectively, this is an outstanding oral argument.‚Äù) Second, I think that people who think AI is going to put a large fraction of programmers out of work are fooling themselves. We‚Äôve just seen that, today, three years after ChatGPT took the world by storm, the number of human programmers is growing as fast as ever. Even the companies that are the biggest boosters of the ‚ÄúAI will replace programmers‚Äù meme are actually aggressively growing, not reducing, their human programmer workforces. So if we extend the question of ‚Äúwhat are our top constraints on software?‚Äù to include not only hardware and power, the #3 long-term constraint is clear: We are chronically short of skilled human programmers. Humans are not being replaced en masse, not most of us; we are being made more productive, and we‚Äôre needed more than ever. As I wrote above: ‚ÄúProgramming is a hot market, and programmers are in long-term high-growth demand.‚Äù [1] It‚Äôs actually great news that Big Tech is spending heavily on power, because the gigawatt capacity we build today is a long-term asset that will keep working for 15 to 20+ years, whether the companies that initially build that capacity survive or get absorbed. That‚Äôs important because it means all the power generation being built out today to satisfy demand in the current ‚ÄúAI bubble‚Äù will continue to be around when the next major demand for compute after AI comes along. See Ben Thompson‚Äôs great writing, such as ‚ÄúThe Benefits of Bubbles‚Äù (Stratechery, November 2025). [2] The Hitchhiker‚Äôs Guide to the Galaxy contains two opposite ideas, both fun but improbable: (1) The problem of being ‚Äútoo compute-constrained‚Äù: Deep Thought, the size of a city, wouldn‚Äôt really be allowed to run for 7.5 million years; you‚Äôd build a million cities. (2) The problem of having ‚Äútoo much excess compute capacity‚Äù: By the time a Marvin with a ‚Äúbrain the size of a planet‚Äù was built, he wouldn‚Äôt really be bored; we‚Äôd already be trying to solve problems the size of the solar system. [3] This is about ‚Äúgeneral-purpose‚Äù coding. Code at the leading specialized edges will always include use of custom DSLs. [4] This means that compiling plain C code (that is in the C/C++ intersection) as C++26 also automatically makes it more correct and more secure. This isn‚Äôt new; compiling C code as C++ and having the C code be more correct has been true since the 1980s. [5] If you‚Äôre my age, you remember when your teacher fretted that letting you use a calculator would harm your education. More of you remember similar angsting about letting students google the internet. Now we see the same fears with AI ‚Äî as if we could stop it or any of those others even if we should. And we shouldn‚Äôt; each time, we re-learn the lesson that teaching students to use such tools should be part of their education because using tools makes us more productive. [6] This is not the same as Wirth‚Äôs Law, that ‚Äúsoftware is getting slower more rapidly than hardware is becoming faster.‚Äù Wirth‚Äôs observation was that the overheads of operating systems and higher-level runtimes and other costly abstractions were becoming ever heavier over time, so that a program to solve the same problem was getting more and more inefficient and soaking up more hardware capacity than it used to; for example, printing ‚ÄúHello world‚Äù really does take far more power and hardware when written in modern Java than it did in Commodore 64 BASIC. No, the key point I‚Äôm making here is very different: that the problems the software is tackling are growing faster than hardware is becoming faster.
